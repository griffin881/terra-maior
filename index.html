Prefatory Note

This monograph proceeds from first principles. It does not presuppose the conclusion it investigates. It applies a single, consistent epistemological framework to all competing models of terrestrial geometry and declines to grant any model preferential immunity from scrutiny. The reader who finds the conclusions uncomfortable is invited to identify the specific premise in the argument that fails — not to invoke authority, consensus, or ridicule as a substitute for counter-argument. Argument terminates in reasons; it does not terminate in headcounts.

The structure is deliberate. Part I constructs the evaluative framework. Part II examines the institutional context in which scientific consensus forms. Part III presents the competing models with formal precision. Parts IV and V put all models under equal pressure. Part VI develops the affirmative case. Part VII states falsification conditions.

Quod demonstrandum erat.

Pars Prima

A RECONSTRUCTION OF EPISTEMOLOGY

This section establishes the evaluative framework to be applied throughout the monograph. It concerns any scientific claim whatsoever and does not refer to any particular domain of empirical inquiry.

§ 1.1 What Counts as Knowledge?

The question of what distinguishes knowledge from mere belief — or from well-supported but ultimately incorrect opinion — is among the oldest problems in philosophy. It is also, for our purposes, the most consequential. If we cannot specify what conditions must be satisfied for a proposition to count as known, we have no principled basis for assessing competing scientific claims.

The JTB Analysis and Its Collapse

The dominant account of knowledge in the Western philosophical tradition, traceable to Plato's Meno and Theaetetus, holds that knowledge is justified true belief (JTB). The analysis has three components: (1) the subject S believes that p; (2) p is in fact true; and (3) S is justified in believing that p. These three conditions were regarded for centuries as jointly sufficient and individually necessary. If you hold a true belief for good reasons, you know it.

Edmund Gettier's 1963 paper "Is Justified True Belief Knowledge?" (Analysis, vol. 23, pp. 121–123) demolished this analysis with two counterexamples that can be replicated at will. The essential structure of a Gettier case is this: S is justified in believing a false proposition p; p entails q; S therefore has justified belief in q; and q turns out to be true — but for entirely independent reasons that have nothing to do with S's justificatory chain. The result is a justified, true belief that no epistemologist would be willing to call knowledge, because the truth of q is entirely accidental relative to S's grounds for believing it.

Gettier's paper forced sixty subsequent years of philosophical repair work. Among the principal proposed amendments: the "no false lemmas" condition (the justification may not rely on any false intermediate belief); the "reliable process" condition (the belief must be produced by a reliable cognitive process); the "causal condition" (the fact that p must stand in an appropriate causal relationship to S's belief that p); and various defeasibility conditions (there must be no true proposition that, if added to S's evidence, would defeat the justification). None of these amendments has commanded universal assent; each faces counterexamples of its own. The JTB programme has not converged, and many epistemologists regard the project as having demonstrated that there is no clean reductive analysis of knowledge in terms of independently specifiable components.

The significance of this failure for empirical science is direct. When a scientist or institution claims to know that the Earth is an oblate spheroid, the JTB framework provides no secure purchase. The justification offered is a complex web of observations, models, auxiliary assumptions, and inference chains. If any node in that web involves the kind of accidental truth that Gettier identifies, the knowledge claim fails — not because the conclusion is necessarily false, but because the epistemic path to it is defective.

Reliabilism and Its Generality Problem

Alvin Goldman's 1979 paper "What Is Justified Belief?" (in George Pappas, ed., Justification and Knowledge, Reidel, pp. 1–23) offered what seemed a promising exit from the Gettier impasse. On Goldman's process reliabilism, a belief is justified if and only if it is the output of a reliable belief-forming process. The processes paradigmatically include perception, memory, introspection, and valid inference. A reliable process is one that tends, over its range of applications, to produce true beliefs. The account has the virtues of naturalizing epistemology (connecting it to empirical psychology) and of avoiding the circularity problems that plague some coherentist and foundationalist alternatives.

Reliabilism, however, faces a problem Goldman himself acknowledged: the generality problem. Any token belief is the output of a particular process event — this very perceptual experience, at this moment, under these conditions. But to assess reliability we must classify that token event under some type of process. And the individuation of process types is radically underdetermined by the theory. The same token event of seeing a distant ship on a hazy day instantiates, simultaneously: vision, daytime vision, long-distance vision, vision under conditions of atmospheric refraction, vision under conditions of an aether gradient, and indefinitely many other process types. Some of these are highly reliable; others are not. Reliabilism provides no principled criterion for selecting the relevant type (see Conee and Feldman, "The Generality Problem for Reliabilism," Philosophical Studies 89 (1998), pp. 1–29).

For the present inquiry, the generality problem is not merely technical. Much of what is claimed as "knowledge" about large-scale terrestrial geometry rests on instrument-mediated observations and model-dependent interpretations. The reliability of GPS receivers, satellite imagery, and geodetic surveys cannot be assessed without first specifying what process type they instantiate — and that specification already embeds theoretical commitments. A GPS receiver that operates on the basis of relativistic corrections is reliable if and only if relativistic mechanics correctly describes the relevant physical processes. To classify its outputs as knowledge, the reliabilist must already have resolved the theoretical dispute that the observation was supposed to adjudicate.

Social Epistemology and the Communal Dimension

Goldman's later work, particularly Knowledge in a Social World (Oxford: Clarendon, 1999), extends reliabilism to the social dimension. The key insight is that beliefs are typically not formed in isolation: testimony, peer review, institutional consensus, and media amplification all function as belief-forming processes. Goldman identifies criteria for evaluating these social processes — veritistic value, speed, distribution — and asks which social practices best serve epistemic goals. Longino's Science as Social Knowledge (Princeton University Press, 1990) adds a critical dimension: scientific knowledge is constituted through the practice of publicly subjecting claims to critical scrutiny, and this practice is itself embedded in power structures, funding regimes, and professional incentives that may distort the outcome.

The significance for this inquiry: scientific consensus is itself a social belief-forming process, and its reliability cannot be assumed. It must be demonstrated — and that demonstration cannot simply cite the consensus as its own warrant without circularity.

The Working Definition

For the purposes of this monograph, a proposition p is treated as known if and only if the following conditions are jointly satisfied:

(K1) p is believed by S. (K2) p is true. (K3) The justification for p is composed of publicly checkable elements — observations, inferences, and auxiliaries — that any competent investigator can independently verify. (K4) The justification is stable under substitution of methodology: the same conclusion is reached by investigators using independent methods, independent instruments, and independent theoretical frameworks. (K5) The justification does not circularly presuppose the conclusion being established.

Condition (K4) is especially demanding. It rules out situations where all roads to the conclusion run through the same theoretical framework, because in such cases the "multiple confirmations" are not independent — they are instances of the same theory confirming itself. Condition (K5) is what philosophers call the requirement of non-circularity or non-question-begging support. As we shall see, some of the most frequently cited evidence for the orthodox model of terrestrial geometry fails precisely this condition.

§ 1.2 Four Modalities of Empirical Access

Not all empirical claims are equal. A claim based on direct unaided observation occupies a different epistemic position than one derived by a chain of model-dependent inference from instrument readings. Distinguishing these modalities is essential to evaluating competing claims about terrestrial geometry.

Direct Observation: The Phenomenological Baseline

Edmund Husserl's phenomenology, particularly his notion of Anschauung ("bringing to intuition") in the Logical Investigations (Halle: Max Niemeyer, 1900–1901) and the Ideas I (1913), identifies direct perceptual experience as the foundational mode of contact with the world. On Husserl's account, something is directly evident when it is given in intuition without inference — when the object itself, rather than a mere representation or sign, is "bodily present" to consciousness.

In the context of empirical science, direct observation provides the phenomenological baseline: the class of facts that are given without interpretive mediation and whose epistemic credentials are therefore most secure. When I observe that a ship's hull disappears before its mast as it recedes, I am seeing something directly. Whether that observation demonstrates the Earth's curvature is an entirely separate question — one that depends on the optical model, the atmospheric model, and the geometry of the surface — but the observation itself is as close to epistemically basic as empirical claims get.

Direct observation's epistemic primacy is, however, limited in scope. The geometry of the entire terrestrial surface cannot be directly observed from any point on it. Claims about the shape of the Earth as a whole are necessarily claims that go beyond what any individual observer can directly see.

Instrument-Mediated Data

A second modality involves instruments: telescopes, interferometers, GPS receivers, mass spectrometers, gravimeters. Instruments extend the range of human observation far beyond its unaided limits. But they do so at a cost: every instrument reading is theory-laden in a particularly deep way. The instrument is itself a physical system whose behavior is described by theories, and interpreting the reading requires those theories to be in place.

Consider the GPS receiver. Its output — a latitude and longitude — is computed from the time-of-flight of radio signals from orbiting satellites. The computation applies corrections for atmospheric delay, satellite clock drift (including relativistic corrections), and Earth's rotation. The final coordinate is not directly observed; it is inferred from raw signal timing data through a chain of models. If any model in the chain is significantly in error, the coordinate is not what it appears to be. The coordinate is accurate relative to the model; whether it corresponds to something called "position on a spheroid" is a further question.

Mathematical Modeling

A third modality, increasingly dominant in modern science, is the mathematical model. The Earth's shape is characterized not by anyone's direct observation but by a computational abstraction: the World Geodetic System 1984 (WGS84), which specifies an oblate spheroid with semi-major axis a = 6,378,137 m and flattening f = 1/298.257223563. No human being has ever directly observed an oblate spheroid with these parameters. The model is constructed by fitting gravitational and geodetic measurements to a parametric family of ellipsoids — and the measurements themselves presuppose the model (or a predecessor to it) to be interpreted.

Morgan and Morrison, in their edited volume Models as Mediators: Perspectives on Natural and Social Science (Cambridge University Press, 1999), argue precisely that models are not direct depictions of the world but "mediating instruments" that stand between theory and data without being straightforwardly reducible to either. Models are constructed, idealized, and fitted; they are not read off from the world.

Institutional Consensus

The fourth modality is the most sociologically potent: the appeal to what scientists, as a professional community, have collectively endorsed. This is the mode of access invoked when one says "NASA confirms that the Earth is round" or "every space agency agrees." The epistemic weight of such claims depends entirely on the reliability of the processes by which institutional consensus forms — and that is precisely what Part II of this monograph examines.

§ 1.3 The Theory-Ladenness of Observation

Hanson and the Seeing-As Structure

Norwood Russell Hanson's Patterns of Discovery: An Inquiry into the Conceptual Foundations of Science (Cambridge University Press, 1958) argues, with careful attention to the phenomenology of perception, that all scientific observation is theory-laden — that what a scientist sees through an instrument or with the naked eye depends on the theoretical framework they bring to the act of seeing.

Hanson's central case is the contrast between Johannes Kepler and Tycho Brahe watching the sunrise. Both are looking at the same physical scene. But Kepler sees "a stationary horizon dipping away from the rising Sun," while Brahe sees "the Sun climbing above a stationary horizon." Their visual experience is, at the level of seeing-as, genuinely different — not merely described differently — because their frameworks for organizing the perceptual field are different. "There is more to seeing than meets the eyeball," Hanson writes (Patterns of Discovery, p. 7).

This thesis has immediate consequences for the present inquiry. An observer trained to see through a spheroidal framework will, when looking at the horizon, organize their visual field differently than one who does not share that framework. The "seeing" of Earth's curvature in photographs, satellite imagery, or through a telephoto lens is not epistemically neutral.

Kuhn's Paradigm-Dependency

Thomas Kuhn's The Structure of Scientific Revolutions (University of Chicago Press, 1962; 2nd ed. 1970) extended Hanson's point into the sociology of science. On Kuhn's account, normal science takes place within a paradigm — a shared exemplar that determines what counts as a problem, what counts as a solution, and what counts as an observation. Scientists in different paradigms, Kuhn argues, are in a sense "living in different worlds" (Structure, p. 150): their perceptions, problems, and standards of evidence are all shaped by the framework they inhabit.

Crucially, Kuhn argues that transitions between paradigms cannot be achieved by the progressive accumulation of evidence within the old framework, because the anomalies that precipitate crisis only appear as anomalies relative to the old paradigm. Competing paradigms are, in Kuhn's term, incommensurable: their standards of evaluation are sufficiently different that there is no neutral ground from which one can be straightforwardly judged superior.

The Duhem-Quine Thesis

The theory-ladenness of observation is given a precise logical formulation in the Duhem-Quine thesis, named for Pierre Duhem's La Théorie Physique: Son objet et sa structure (Paris: Chevalier & Rivière, 1906; translated as The Aim and Structure of Physical Theory, Princeton University Press, 1954) and Willard Van Orman Quine's "Two Dogmas of Empiricism" (Philosophical Review 60 (1951), pp. 20–43).

In its precise form, the thesis holds the following: no hypothesis H is tested in isolation against observation. Any apparent test of H is actually a test of the conjunction of H with an auxiliary hypothesis set A = {a₁, a₂, ..., aₙ}, which includes background theories, instrument theories, claims about experimental conditions, and indefinitely many other elements. When an observation O is made and O is inconsistent with H ∧ A, logic alone does not tell us whether to reject H or to modify one or more members of A. The "blame" for the failure can be distributed in any way across the conjunction.

Duhem stated the point sharply: "Physical theory is not a mere mechanical linking together of laws. In it, all the parts are connected with each other, all the parts lend support to each other, and the chain thus formed is attached at several points to the totality of physical experiment" (The Aim and Structure of Physical Theory, p. 294).

The consequence for the present monograph is direct. Every piece of evidence cited in favor of the spheroidal model is evidence for a conjunction: (spheroidal model) ∧ (classical mechanics) ∧ (optics model) ∧ (atmosphere model) ∧ (instrument theory) ∧ ... . Any of these conjuncts can be modified when the conjunction faces apparent disconfirmation. This is not a defect in the methodology of the spheroidal programme — it is a structural feature of all scientific testing.

§ 1.4 The Limits of Empiricism and Positivism

The Failure of the Verification Criterion

Logical positivism, developed by the Vienna Circle in the 1920s and 1930s and given canonical expression in A.J. Ayer's Language, Truth and Logic (London: Gollancz, 1936), held that a meaningful proposition must be either analytic (true by definition) or empirically verifiable. Any proposition that is neither is cognitively meaningless — not false, but without truth value.

The programme collapsed under self-referential pressure. The verification criterion itself is neither analytic nor empirically verifiable: no observation could confirm or disconfirm the statement "only analytic and verifiable statements are meaningful." The criterion therefore fails its own test. Ayer himself acknowledged this in the preface to the second edition (1946), and subsequent attempts to formulate a satisfactory version of the criterion — by Carnap, Hempel, and others — all encountered decisive counterexamples. The positivist programme is now generally regarded as having failed (see Hempel, "Empiricist Criteria of Cognitive Significance," Aspects of Scientific Explanation, New York: Free Press, 1965, pp. 101–122).

Popper's Falsificationism and Its Limits

Karl Popper's Logik der Forschung (Vienna: Julius Springer, 1934; translated as The Logic of Scientific Discovery, London: Hutchinson, 1959) proposed falsifiability rather than verifiability as the demarcation criterion for science. A hypothesis is scientific if and only if it is in principle falsifiable — if there exists an observation that, if made, would contradict the hypothesis. This criterion has the virtue of applying to genuinely substantive claims while excluding metaphysical claims.

But Popper's programme faces precisely the Duhem-Quine problem. If no hypothesis is tested in isolation, then no observation falsifies a hypothesis alone — it falsifies the hypothesis together with its auxiliaries. And since the auxiliaries can always be modified, any hypothesis can be protected from falsification indefinitely, at some cost in auxiliary complexity. Popper was aware of this and responded by insisting that scientists should adhere to a methodological rule not to modify auxiliaries in purely ad hoc ways — ways that increase no independent testable content. But this rule is itself not falsifiable, and its application in practice requires judgment that varies among investigators.

Imre Lakatos, in "Falsification and the Methodology of Scientific Research Programmes" (in Lakatos and Musgrave, eds., Criticism and the Growth of Knowledge, Cambridge University Press, 1970, pp. 91–196), refined Popper's account by shifting the unit of appraisal from individual hypotheses to research programmes. A research programme has a hard core of central commitments protected from falsification by methodological fiat, and a protective belt of auxiliary hypotheses that absorb observational pressure. A programme is progressive if the successive modifications of the protective belt generate novel, independently testable predictions that are confirmed; it is degenerating if the modifications are merely ad hoc — designed only to protect the hard core from already-known anomalies without generating new predictions.

Paul Feyerabend's Against Method (London: New Left Books, 1975) pushed further, arguing that no single methodological rule has been universally followed by successful scientists, and that the history of science shows that rule-violation has often been necessary for progress. Feyerabend's epistemological anarchism — "anything goes" — is deliberately provocative, but its underlying point is substantive: the normative methodology of science cannot be derived from the history of its actual practice.

Van Fraassen's Constructive Empiricism

Bas van Fraassen's The Scientific Image (Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1980) offers a carefully articulated alternative to scientific realism that takes the Duhem-Quine problem seriously without retreating into skepticism. Van Fraassen's constructive empiricism holds that the aim of science is not truth but empirical adequacy: a theory is empirically adequate if what it says about observable phenomena is true. Acceptance of a theory need not involve belief in the truth of its theoretical claims — only in its adequacy over the range of observables.

The distinction between truth and empirical adequacy is pivotal. Two theories that are empirically equivalent — that make all the same observational predictions — cannot be distinguished by empirical evidence alone. A preference for one over the other is, on van Fraassen's account, a pragmatic choice, not an epistemic one. As van Fraassen notes: "the assertion of empirical adequacy is a great deal weaker than the assertion of truth, and the restraint to acceptance delivers us from metaphysics" (The Scientific Image, p. 69).

The Pessimistic Meta-Induction

Larry Laudan's "A Confutation of Convergent Realism" (Philosophy of Science 48 (1981), pp. 19–49) presents what has become one of the most discussed arguments against scientific realism. The pessimistic meta-induction runs as follows: if we survey the history of science, we find that virtually every theory that was once regarded as our best confirmed theory has subsequently been abandoned or radically revised. The geocentric model of the solar system, phlogiston theory, caloric theory of heat, the luminiferous aether, Newtonian absolute space and time — all were once the established scientific consensus; all were replaced. The inductive generalization from this historical record suggests that our current theories are similarly likely to be abandoned. What grounds, then, do we have for supposing that present-day consensus models are approximately true?

Laudan's point is not that current theories are false — it is that the inference from "well-confirmed theory" to "approximately true theory" is undercut by the historical record. The scientific realist's key move — from empirical success to approximate truth via inference to the best explanation — relies on a track record that does not exist.

For the present inquiry, the pessimistic meta-induction applies directly to the history of cosmological and geodetic models. The progression from flat to spherical to oblate spheroidal to (in modern precision geodesy) a geoid of irregular shape — where the true "shape of the Earth" is given by an equipotential surface that cannot be directly observed and must be continuously recomputed — does not obviously represent convergence on a unique truth. It may represent progressive precision within a single paradigm, or it may represent what Laudan calls "theoretical turbulence."

§ 1.5 Models and Reality

Cartwright on the Mendacity of Physical Laws

Nancy Cartwright's How the Laws of Physics Lie (Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1983) advances a striking thesis: the fundamental laws of physics — the laws that appear in theoretical frameworks like quantum mechanics and general relativity — are systematically false. They are true only of highly idealized objects in theoretical models. The laws that are actually true are phenomenological laws — low-level, context-specific generalizations that describe what happens in concrete situations. The fundamental laws, by contrast, "get the right results in the right models," but the models systematically distort the underlying reality by abstracting from the disorderly complexity of actual phenomena.

Cartwright's argument proceeds through a series of worked examples. Newton's law of gravity, \( F = Gm_1m_2/r^2 \), holds only for point masses in a vacuum with no other forces. The moment any real-world complication is introduced — extended bodies, electromagnetic forces, atmospheric drag — the law must be supplemented, modified, or replaced with phenomenological corrections. The phenomenological laws that actually describe the behavior of a billiard ball rolling across a table cannot be derived from the fundamental law; they are constructed case by case through what Cartwright calls the "simulacrum account of explanation" (How the Laws of Physics Lie, p. 17).

For the present inquiry, Cartwright's point applies with full force to the models used to characterize the shape of the Earth. The oblate spheroid of WGS84 is not "the actual shape of the Earth" — it is a theoretical model, a convenient mathematical surface whose parameters are fitted to a particular class of geodetic measurements within a particular theoretical framework. The geoid — the more accurate potential surface used in precision geodesy — is not a simple geometric shape at all; it is a complex, computationally derived surface that changes as new measurements are incorporated and as the theoretical models used to interpret them evolve.

The Semantic View and Model-Dependent Realism

The "semantic view" of scientific theories, developed by Patrick Suppes, Frederick Suppe, and others, holds that a scientific theory is not a set of sentences but a family of models — mathematical structures that are intended to be isomorphic to real systems (Suppe, ed., The Structure of Scientific Theories, Urbana: University of Illinois Press, 1974). On the semantic view, a theory is "true" if one of its models is structurally isomorphic to the actual world. But since no model is literally identical to the world — models always involve idealization and approximation — the question of which model is "correct" cannot be answered by direct comparison.

Stephen Hawking and Leonard Mlodinow's The Grand Design (New York: Bantam Books, 2010) coins the phrase "model-dependent realism" for the view that "there is no picture- or theory-independent concept of reality" and that "it is pointless to ask whether a model is real, only whether it agrees with observation" (The Grand Design, p. 46). If model-dependent realism is right, then the question "what is the true shape of the Earth?" is not a well-formed question in isolation from a specification of which model we are using and for what purposes. Different models may be better or worse for different purposes without there being any uniquely correct model.

§ 1.6 The Evaluation Framework

This section states, explicitly and formally, the criteria that will be applied to every empirical claim examined in subsequent parts of this monograph. These criteria do not embody skepticism; they embody the standards of rigorous empirical reasoning.

(a) Modality of Access

Every empirical claim about terrestrial geometry will be classified according to the modality through which it is accessed: direct observation (D), instrument-mediated data (I), mathematical model (M), or institutional consensus (C). Claims of type D carry the highest intrinsic epistemic weight and the smallest burden of auxiliary justification. Claims of type C carry the lowest intrinsic weight and require that the social processes by which the consensus was formed be independently validated.

(b) Auxiliary Requirements

Every claim will be evaluated with respect to the auxiliary hypothesis set required to derive it from raw data. The larger and more recondite the auxiliary set, the weaker the claim's independent evidential force. In particular, a claim that requires as an auxiliary the conclusion it is intended to establish is epistemically void.

(c) Empirical Uniqueness or Underdetermination

Every claim will be assessed for empirical uniqueness: does the observation uniquely support the orthodox model, or is it equally compatible with alternative models? A claim that is compatible with multiple models provides no differential support for any of them; it fails to discriminate.

(d) Lakatosian Progressive vs. Degenerating Test

For each major programme, we ask: have the auxiliary adjustments made in response to anomalies generated novel independent predictions, or have they been purely protective? A pattern of purely protective adjustments, without novel confirmed predictions, is the signature of a degenerating programme in Lakatos's sense.

(e) Circularity Test

No claim is admissible as evidence for the orthodox model if the claim's evidential force depends on assuming the orthodox model's framework. This is the no-begging-the-question condition. It is more frequently violated in practice than textbooks suggest.

These five criteria constitute the evaluative spine of this monograph. Every section of Parts III through VI will apply them systematically.

Pars Secunda

THE SOCIAL PRODUCTION OF SCIENTIFIC CONSENSUS

This part analyzes the institutional and sociological mechanisms by which scientific consensus forms and stabilizes. The analysis is structural, not conspiratorial. It draws on mainstream sociology of science and the philosophy of scientific practice.

1§ 2.1 Paradigms, Anomalies, and Inertia

Normal Science and Its Resistance to Challenge

Kuhn's Structure of Scientific Revolutions distinguishes between normal science — the routine puzzle-solving activity that takes place within an established paradigm — and the anomaly-accumulation and paradigm-shift dynamics of scientific revolutions. Normal science is, by design, deeply conservative. Its practitioners are selected and trained to work within the paradigm, not to question it. The paradigm defines what counts as a legitimate research question, a recognizable result, and a valid method of inquiry. Anomalies — observations that resist accommodation within the paradigm — are initially set aside, attributed to experimental error, or addressed by minor adjustments to auxiliary hypotheses. The threshold for taking an anomaly seriously enough to threaten the paradigm is very high, because the entire research community's investment — in methods, training, instruments, publications, and careers — is tied to the paradigm's continued viability.

The Continental Drift Case

The reception of Alfred Wegener's theory of continental drift, which he first proposed in 1912 (Die Entstehung der Kontinente und Ozeane, Braunschweig: Friedrich Vieweg & Sohn, 1912), is the canonical illustration of paradigm resistance. The geological and geophysical establishment rejected drift for over fifty years. The evidence Wegener assembled — the jigsaw-fit of continental coastlines, the matching of fossil fauna across the Atlantic, the continuation of geological structures across continental margins — was substantial and visible to any competent observer. But the mechanism Wegener proposed (centrifugal force and tidal friction) was implausible, and more fundamentally, the proposal violated the basic assumption of the then-current geological paradigm: that continents and ocean basins were permanent features of the Earth's crust.

The tide turned only in the late 1950s and 1960s, with the development of paleomagnetism and the discovery of mid-ocean ridge symmetry, which provided both a mechanism (seafloor spreading) and an independent confirmation methodology (magnetic stratigraphy). The lesson is that a correct empirical claim — that the continents had drifted — was actively suppressed by institutional consensus for half a century. The scientists who rejected Wegener were not villains; they were operating normally within their paradigm. But their consensus was wrong.

Fleck's Thought Collectives

Ludwik Fleck's Genesis and Development of a Scientific Fact (Basel: Benno Schwabe, 1935; translated, Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 1979) developed an independent account of scientific community dynamics that anticipated Kuhn's by nearly three decades. Fleck's central concept is the Denkkollektiv (thought collective): a community of scientists who share a Denkstil (thought style) — a set of background assumptions, conceptual frameworks, and epistemic practices that are so thoroughly internalized as to be invisible to their practitioners.

For Fleck, a "scientific fact" is not simply a neutral observation but a stabilized social achievement: a claim that has passed through the "thought collective's" filters and been incorporated into the shared Denkstil. Facts are produced, not discovered. This does not make them arbitrary — Fleck is not a relativist — but it does mean that the social processes by which facts are constituted must be examined before the facts themselves can be taken as self-standing evidence.

2§ 2.2 Institutional Structure of Modern Science

Peer Review as Social Filter

Peer review is widely cited as the mechanism that distinguishes scientific from non-scientific knowledge. The claim is that peer review subjects findings to scrutiny by independent experts and thereby filters out error. This is an idealized picture. In practice, peer review is performed by practitioners within the same paradigm as the authors being reviewed — practitioners who share the same background assumptions, employ the same methods, and have the same career incentives. Peer review screens for technical competence and conformity to paradigm standards; it does not and cannot screen for paradigm-level error.

“The approximate replication rate across 100 high-profile findings in social and cognitive psychology was 36%.”

Moreover, peer review is conducted under conditions of substantial conflict of interest. Reviewers are typically competitors of the authors; they have reputational stakes in the outcome; they are unpaid volunteers who devote limited time to each review. The result is a social filtering process that is more reliable at excluding technical errors and methodological non-conformity than at detecting fundamental conceptual error. A proposal that challenges the paradigm's hard core is, almost by definition, likely to be rejected as non-credible by reviewers trained within the paradigm.

Grant Funding as Directional Force

Modern scientific research is largely funded by governmental agencies (NSF, NASA, ESF, etc.) and private foundations whose priorities are determined by advisory boards composed of established practitioners within existing paradigms. Funding flows toward projects that are legible within the existing framework — projects that address well-defined technical puzzles rather than foundational assumptions. The consequence is structural: research that challenges foundational assumptions is systematically defunded, not because of any deliberate suppression but because it does not match the categories in which funding is allocated.

This creates a self-reinforcing cycle. The questions that get funded are the questions that get answered. The answers that accumulate are answers to questions defined within the existing paradigm. The paradigm grows more elaborate and more entrenched. Alternative frameworks, even if initially promising, cannot accumulate the critical mass of results needed to challenge the paradigm because they cannot access the resources needed to generate those results.

Tenure, Promotion, and Career Incentives

Academic tenure and promotion systems reward productivity within the prevailing paradigm. Publication in high-impact journals, citation accumulation, grant acquisition, and mentorship of graduate students who go on to academic positions — all of these are tied to working within the established framework. A researcher who challenges the paradigm's hard core risks not being published in top journals, not receiving grants, and not achieving tenure. These are not trivial penalties; they represent the effective end of an academic career.

The structural consequence is clear: the career incentive structure of modern academic science powerfully selects for paradigm conformity and against paradigm challenge. This does not mean that paradigm challenges never succeed — clearly they do, as the history of science demonstrates — but it does mean that the threshold for a successful paradigm challenge is enormously high, and that many challenges that would have succeeded in a more epistemically open environment are suppressed before they can accumulate the evidence needed to make their case.

Citation Networks and h-Index Metrics

The quantification of scientific reputation through citation metrics (h-index, impact factor, citation count) has added a new layer of epistemic enclosure. High-citation work tends to be work that engages with the central problems of a paradigm in ways that practitioners find useful; it tends to receive citations because other practitioners are building on it within the same framework. Dissenting work, by contrast, tends to be cited only polemically — to be refuted — and such citations often fail to count in the metrics, or count less.

The result is that the "reputation economy" of science has internal dynamics that reinforce consensus independently of truth. A proposition that is widely cited is not thereby more likely to be true; it is more likely to be central to a paradigm. Centrality to a paradigm and truth are different properties.

3§ 2.3 The Stabilization of Error

Mertonian Norms and the Counter-Norms

Robert Merton's "The Normative Structure of Science" (1942; in Merton, The Sociology of Science, Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 1973, pp. 267–278) identified four normative principles that ideally govern scientific practice: communalism (findings are shared), universalism (claims are evaluated on impersonal criteria), disinterestedness (scientists have no personal stake in findings), and organized skepticism (all claims are subject to critical scrutiny). These norms constitute the aspirational self-image of science.

“Norms and counter-norms operate simultaneously; individual scientists navigate both.”

Ian Mitroff's empirical study of NASA Apollo scientists ("Norms and Counter-Norms in a Select Group of the Apollo Moon Scientists," American Sociological Review 39 (1974), pp. 579–595) found that the Mertonian norms coexist with a set of counter-norms that are equally operative in practice: solitariness (possessing priority over discoveries), particularism (evaluating claims by reference to the author's reputation and affiliation), interestedness (having personal and career stakes in findings), and organized dogmatism (defending one's theoretical commitments against criticism). These counter-norms are not pathological — they are structurally necessary features of competitive scientific careers. But their existence means that the Mertonian self-image of science radically misrepresents actual practice.

The Replication Crisis

The most dramatic recent evidence for the systematic production of error within established scientific practice comes from the replication crisis in psychology and medicine. The Open Science Collaboration's 2015 study ("Estimating the Reproducibility of Psychological Science," Science 349 (2015), aac4716) attempted to replicate 100 published psychological studies. Only 36% of replications produced statistically significant results. Replication effect sizes were on average half the magnitude of original effects. 97% of original studies had significant results. The contrast is stark: nearly all original studies found what they looked for; fewer than half of replications found the same thing.

John Ioannidis's "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False" (PLOS Medicine 2 (2005), e124) applied a probabilistic analysis to demonstrate that, given typical statistical power levels, prior probabilities, and selective publication practices, the majority of statistically significant findings in medical literature are false positives. Ioannidis's calculation is sensitive to parameters, and the conclusion has been debated; but subsequent replication studies in cancer biology (Errington et al., "Investigating the Replicability of Preclinical Cancer Biology," eLife 10 (2021), e71601, which found that only 50 of 193 experiments from high-profile cancer papers replicated) have broadly confirmed the thrust of his analysis.

The meta-point is epistemologically significant. Peer-reviewed publication in high-impact journals — the gold standard of scientific credibility — has proven to be a far less reliable indicator of truth than the normative self-image of science suggested. If this rate of non-replication holds even approximately across empirical science, then the prima facie evidential weight of published findings must be substantially discounted.

The File Drawer Problem and Publication Bias

The file drawer problem, identified by Rosenthal ("The File Drawer Problem and Tolerance for Null Results," Psychological Bulletin 86 (1979), pp. 638–641) refers to the systematic non-publication of negative and null results. Studies that fail to find a predicted effect are less likely to be submitted for publication, less likely to be accepted when submitted, and less likely to be prominently positioned when published. The published literature therefore represents a non-random sample of the total evidence: it over-represents positive findings.

For the present inquiry, this matters specifically: the absence of published positive evidence for an alternative model of terrestrial geometry cannot be taken as evidence that such evidence does not exist. Negative results — experiments that failed to confirm the orthodox model — may be systematically absent from the literature not because the experiments were not performed but because they were not published.

Collins on the Experimenter's Regress

Harry Collins's "The Seven Sexes: A Study in the Sociology of a Phenomenon" (Sociology 9 (1975), pp. 205–224) and his more extensive work Changing Order: Replication and Induction in Scientific Practice (London: Sage, 1985) introduce the concept of the experimenter's regress. The problem is this: to determine whether an experiment has been performed correctly, one must know what the correct result is; but to determine what the correct result is, one must trust the experiment. The regress is circular.

In practice, the circle is broken not by logical necessity but by social negotiation: scientists come to agree, through a process of community judgment that involves reputation, authority, and paradigm loyalty, that a particular experimental setup is correct and its results are reliable. The criteria for this judgment are not transparent or algorithmic; they are embedded in the tacit knowledge and social organization of the relevant specialty. Collins's analysis implies that experimental results cannot be read off from the physical facts alone; they are always already socially interpreted.

4§ 2.4 How Model-Dependent Interpretation Propagates

The ΛCDM Case: Anomaly Absorption Without Resolution

The cosmological context within which the orthodox spheroidal model of the Earth is embedded provides an instructive illustration of how a degenerating programme can persist under conditions of severe anomalous pressure. The Λ Cold Dark Matter (ΛCDM) model of cosmology — which incorporates the spheroidal Earth model as a component — currently faces multiple unresolved tensions that have been openly acknowledged in the specialist literature:

“Every anomaly becomes an occasion for protective auxiliary.”

The Hubble tension: the value of the Hubble constant H₀ inferred from the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) using the Planck satellite (H₀ = 67.4 ± 0.5 km s⁻¹ Mpc⁻¹; Planck Collaboration 2020) is in tension with local distance-ladder measurements (H₀ = 73.2 ± 1.3 km s⁻¹ Mpc⁻¹; Riess et al., Astrophysical Journal Letters 934 (2022), L7), with a discrepancy exceeding 5σ — a level that in particle physics would constitute discovery-class significance for new physics. Recent analyses suggest this tension may exceed 7σ across independent methods (arXiv:2601.01525, 2026).

The S₈ tension: the parameter S₈ ≡ σ₈√(Ωm/0.3) — quantifying the amplitude of large-scale structure — is inconsistent between CMB-inferred and weak-lensing-measured values at a level of 2–3σ.

The lithium problem: standard Big Bang nucleosynthesis, a core component of the ΛCDM framework, predicts a primordial abundance of lithium-7 approximately three times higher than observed. No accepted solution exists.

The dark matter problem: 26.6% of the universe's energy content is attributed to dark matter in the ΛCDM model, yet no dark matter particle has been directly detected despite decades of dedicated experimental search (XENON1T, LUX-ZEPLIN, and others).

The dark energy problem: 68.5% of the energy content is attributed to dark energy (the cosmological constant Λ), for which there is no physical theory. The observed value of Λ differs from quantum field theory's vacuum energy prediction by approximately 120 orders of magnitude — the largest discrepancy in theoretical physics.

Each of these tensions has been addressed by auxiliary modifications to the ΛCDM model: interacting dark energy, modified gravity, new physics in the early universe, and so on. The modification pattern fits Lakatos's description of a degenerating programme: protective adjustments are made to the hard core (or its immediate vicinity) in response to anomalies, without generating novel independent predictions that have been confirmed. The programme has not been abandoned because it has no confirmed replacement.

Textbooks and the Standardization of Interpretation

Kuhn observed (Structure of Scientific Revolutions, p. 137) that science textbooks characteristically present the current consensus as if it were arrived at by simple, cumulative observation and straightforward inference. The historical path — the anomalies, the controversies, the alternatives that were rejected — is erased. The result is a distorted picture of why the current consensus holds, which makes paradigm revision psychologically harder.

This observation applies with full force to the teaching of geodesy, astronomy, and physics. Introductory curricula present the spherical/oblate spheroidal Earth as an established observational fact, when it is better understood as the central commitment of a long-dominant research programme that has faced, and continues to face, significant evidential challenges.

5§ 2.5 What This Section Does Not Claim

This section has not argued that scientists lie, fabricate data, or conspire to suppress the truth. The analysis has been structural, not motivational. Scientists, on average, are competent, honest, and well-intentioned. The structural tendencies identified — paradigm conservatism, funding directionality, career incentives for conformity, the file drawer problem, the experimenter's regress — do not require bad faith. They are the predictable emergent properties of a competitive institution with finite resources operating under incomplete information.

The section does claim the following: given the structural features of modern science, there is a non-negligible probability that any given scientific consensus is institutionally stabilized even when not uniquely forced by the available evidence. This probability is not zero; the replication crisis establishes it as meaningfully positive. The question to be posed in the rest of this monograph is whether the specific consensus about terrestrial geometry falls on the forced side or the institutionally stabilized side of this distribution.

Pars Tertia

A FORMAL PRESENTATION OF MULTIPLE MODELS

This section presents five competing models of terrestrial geometry with technical precision. No model is given preferential treatment. The aim is to characterize each model's predictive structure, geometric commitments, and auxiliary requirements so that they can be evaluated against the framework of Part I.

A

Model A: The Oblate Spheroid (Orthodox)

Geometry

Model A specifies the Earth's shape as an oblate spheroid — a solid of revolution generated by rotating an ellipse about its minor axis. The defining parameters in the World Geodetic System 1984 (WGS84) are:

  • Semi-major axis (equatorial radius): a = 6,378,137.0 m
  • Flattening: f = 1/298.257223563
  • Semi-minor axis (polar radius): b = a(1-f) = 6,356,752.3 m
  • First eccentricity: e² = 1 - (b/a)² ≈ 0.00669438

The departure from a perfect sphere is small (approximately 21 km difference between equatorial and polar radii) but not negligible for precision geodesy, satellite orbit computation, or gravity modeling. The model is embedded in a broader framework: the Earth is a body in the solar system, orbiting the Sun, rotating about its polar axis with a period of approximately 86,400 seconds, with the Sun at approximately 1.496 × 10¹¹ m (1 AU) from the Earth's center.

Physics

The physical framework is Newtonian mechanics with General Relativistic corrections. Newton's law of universal gravitation:

\[ F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \]

provides the dominant interaction. GR corrections are required at the level of precision needed for GPS and satellite geodesy. The Standard Model of particle physics is required for the interpretation of nuclear clocks. Quantum mechanics is required for the GPS clock correction term.

Predictive Capability

Model A predicts, in principle:

  • The variation of gravitational acceleration g with latitude and altitude
  • The trajectory of long-range ballistic projectiles (Coriolis deflection)
  • The behavior of Foucault pendulums
  • The timing of solar and lunar eclipses
  • The pattern of stellar parallax
  • The geometry of circumnavigation routes
  • The behavior of GPS satellites and receivers
  • The structure of tides

Auxiliary Hypotheses Required for Observational Fit

The observational adequacy of Model A requires the following auxiliary hypotheses, among others:

A1. Dark matter (26.6% of total energy density; no direct detection) A2. Dark energy / cosmological constant Λ (68.5% of total energy density; no physical theory) A3. An inflationary field in the early universe (no detected inflaton particle) A4. The incompatibility between general relativity and quantum mechanics does not affect any of the predictions being made (an assumption, not a result) A5. Atmospheric refraction models are correct to the required precision for optical observations A6. The geoid (the actual equipotential surface, as opposed to the reference spheroid) is correctly modeled by the EGM2008 or similar potential models A7. Satellite clocks, receivers, and signal propagation models are correct to nanosecond precision

The presence of A1-A3 is particularly significant: the cosmological framework within which Model A is fully embedded requires hypothetical entities that have never been independently detected. This is not a decisive objection — auxiliary hypotheses routinely invoke undetected entities — but it is a significant entry in the auxiliary cost register.

B

Model B: Neo-Tychonic (Geo-Heliocentric)

Geometry

The Neo-Tychonic model was proposed by Tycho Brahe in 1588 (De Mundi Aetherei Recentioribus Phaenomenis, Uraniborg). On this model, the Earth is stationary at the center of the solar system; the Sun and Moon orbit the Earth; the other planets orbit the Sun. The geometry of planetary motions is identical to the Copernican heliocentric model under a coordinate transformation; the two models are observationally equivalent for all observations accessible in the 17th century.

Physics

In its 17th-century form, the Tychonic model did not have a coherent dynamics — it was descriptive. Modern neo-Tychonic models have been developed that are dynamically equivalent to the heliocentric model within a non-inertial reference frame centered on the Earth. In such frames, fictitious forces (Coriolis, centrifugal) appear as actual forces; the dynamics are formally identical to Newtonian mechanics in the heliocentric frame, transformed to the Earth's frame.

Predictive Capability

For all geocentrically accessible observations — planetary positions, eclipse timing, precession — the Neo-Tychonic model is empirically equivalent to the heliocentric model by the equivalence of reference frames. The stellar parallax observation (a shift of approximately 0.76 arcseconds for the nearest star, α Centauri, over six months) can be accommodated by the stellar sphere moving in the Tychonic frame; this requires very large stellar distances but does not produce an observational inconsistency.

Auxiliary Hypotheses Required

B1. Earth is in a preferred reference frame (violation of the Copernican principle) B2. The very large stellar distances implied by the Tychonic frame and the absence of Coriolis-independent frame effects (Sagnac effect, Earth-rotation dependent experiments) require separate treatment

Model B has largely historical significance but demonstrates an important point: two geometrically incompatible descriptions of the solar system can be empirically equivalent for a substantial class of observations.

C

Model C: Greater Earth / Bounded Disc

Geometry

Model C proposes that the terrestrial surface is a flat or very weakly convex disc, bounded by an ice frontier corresponding to what orthodox cartography calls Antarctica. The disc is centered on the geographic North Pole. The equator is a circle on the disc approximately 20,037 km from the center; the "South Pole" of orthodox geography corresponds to some location on or near the bounding ice frontier. The radius of the disc to its bounding frontier is approximately 20,037 km.

This model is the oldest cosmological model in the documented record of human civilizations. The Akkadian conception of the world (ki — land — as a disc floating on primordial water, surrounded by the cosmic ocean), the Homeric worldview (Iliad XVIII, the Shield of Achilles: the world-ocean surrounding the disc of earth), the Hesiodic cosmogony (Theogony, ~700 BCE), the Vedic Bhu-mandala (the earth-circle), and the Norse Midgard (the middle enclosure) all independently converge on a disc model bounded by water or ice. These convergences do not constitute evidence — they may reflect a universal cognitive bias or a common pre-theoretical interpretation of local topography — but they establish that the disc model is the pre-theoretically natural description of terrestrial experience.

Physics

On Model C, gravity — insofar as it is an independent force rather than a consequence of geometry (as in GR) — acts uniformly downward, perpendicular to the plane of the disc. The magnitude of g is approximately 9.8 m s⁻² everywhere on the surface. Atmospheric pressure, tidal phenomena, and thermodynamics operate within the disc plane; the edges are bounded by the ice frontier. There is no void "below" the disc; the disc has infinite depth in the downward direction, or is bounded at some depth by a substrate.

Celestial Bodies

On Model C as most fully developed:

  • The Sun is a local luminary at an altitude of approximately 3,000–5,000 km above the disc surface, with a disc diameter of approximately 50 km, traversing a spiral path above the disc centered on the North Pole.
  • The Moon is a similar local luminary, self-illuminating, at a comparable altitude.
  • The stars form a celestial canopy rotating about the polar axis overhead.

Predictive Capability

Model C predicts:

  • Sunrise and sunset through perspective foreshortening as the Sun spirals toward and away from the observer's position on the disc
  • The midnight sun at high northern latitudes (where the Sun's spiral path remains above the horizon) and polar night near the outer edges (ice frontier region)
  • The appearance of the horizon as flat at eye level everywhere
  • Tidal patterns through a non-gravitational mechanism (electromagnetic interaction between the Moon and ocean surface)
  • Stellar rotation about a single polar axis (consistent with northern hemisphere observation but requiring modification for the claimed southern hemisphere)

Auxiliary Hypotheses Required

C1. The celestial bodies are local luminaries at finite altitude, not distant objects; their apparent angular sizes must remain roughly constant (but see § 6.4 for the Sun's apparent diameter variation) C2. An account of eclipse mechanics that does not invoke a spherical Earth shadow (see § 4.5) C3. An account of stellar behavior in the southern part of the disc C4. The behavior of long-distance radio and radar propagation must be accommodated within the flat-surface framework

D

Model D: Concave Earth / Hollow Earth

Geometry

The concave earth model, most fully articulated in the Koreshan cosmology developed by Cyrus Teed (1870) and given mathematical expression by Ernst Morrow and K.E. Neupert in the early 20th century, proposes that we live on the interior surface of a sphere. The Earth is not a convex spheroid with an exterior surface; it is a hollow sphere, and we live on the inside. In this geometry, "up" is toward the center of the sphere, and "down" is toward the shell.

The radius of the sphere in Teed's model is approximately 6,371 km — identical to the conventional mean radius of the orthodox model. The geometry is obtained from the orthodox model by an inversion transformation: the mapping r → R²/r, where R is the radius of the sphere of inversion. Under this transformation, every ray that would travel from the Earth's surface outward into space in the orthodox model is "inverted" to a curved ray that re-enters the surface. Straight lines in the orthodox model become curved arcs in the inverted model; the curvature of light paths "compensates" for the apparent anomaly.

Karl Neupert's optical experiments (reported in Gerloff, Hohlweltlehre, Berlin, 1933) claimed to demonstrate, using long-baseline laser observations, that the surface curves upward rather than downward — consistent with the concave earth model. The experiments have not been independently replicated to modern standards.

Physics

The physics of Model D requires that light travels in curved paths (arcs curving toward the axis of the sphere, rather than straight lines). This is not impossible in principle; general relativity shows that gravity curves light paths, and a sufficiently strong uniform field could curve all light paths into identical arcs. The required curvature would need to be precisely calibrated to explain all optical observations.

Predictive Capability

For optical observations, Model D is empirically equivalent to Model A under the inversion transformation. This equivalence was independently noted by the physicist P.M.S. Blackett in informal discussions and has been formalized in the mathematical analysis of conformal mappings. Every observation that supports Model A in principle supports Model D equally, since the two models are geometrically related by an invertible mathematical transformation.

Auxiliary Hypotheses Required

D1. Light travels in precise arcs whose curvature matches the shell radius D2. All other physical interactions (gravity, electromagnetism, particle propagation) are similarly modified to be consistent with the inversion

Model D is the philosophically most interesting alternative to Model A precisely because of its empirical equivalence: it demonstrates that even the most basic geometric claim about the Earth's surface — concave or convex — is not uniquely determined by optical observation.

E

Model E: Simulated / Computational Reality

Geometry

On Model E, "geometry" is not a property of a mind-independent physical world but a feature of the computational substrate that generates the appearance of a physical world. Nick Bostrom's simulation argument ("Are You Living in a Computer Simulation?", Philosophical Quarterly 53 (2003), pp. 243–255) establishes that at least one of the following must be true: (1) nearly all civilizations go extinct before reaching a "post-human" computational level; (2) nearly all post-human civilizations have no interest in running ancestor simulations; (3) we are almost certainly living in a computer simulation. Bostrom argues that given the computational resources available to a sufficiently advanced civilization, running a large number of high-resolution simulations of ancestor civilizations would be trivial, and the probabilistic argument for simulation is therefore strong.

Brian Whitworth's "The Physical World as a Virtual Reality" (arXiv:0801.0337 (2008)) develops the simulation hypothesis as a positive research programme, noting that quantum mechanics — with its discreteness, superposition, and observer-dependence — is more naturally explained by a computational substrate than by any classical physical ontology.

For the Present Inquiry

Model E is included not because it is straightforwardly advocated here but because its inclusion clarifies a point about the scope of underdetermination. If Model E is coherent, then every piece of evidence for every other model is evidence only for the appearance of that model within the simulation, not for anything beyond the simulation itself. Model E is thus an extreme case of van Fraassen's distinction between empirical adequacy and truth: a simulation could be empirically adequate for any observation while having no commitment to any particular underlying geometry.

§ 3.6 Evaluation by the Part I Framework

Applying the five criteria of § 1.6 to the five models:

Criterion (a): Modality of Access

Every model's support rests primarily on instrument-mediated data (I) and mathematical modeling (M), with institutional consensus (C) playing a significant amplifying role. No model is directly observable in its global extent from any terrestrial standpoint. Model A claims the highest diversity of indirect support; Model C claims the highest degree of alignment with direct, unaided terrestrial observation.

Criterion (b): Auxiliary Requirements

Model A requires the largest auxiliary set: dark matter, dark energy, inflation, GR-QM compatibility assumption, precision atmosphere models, and geoid models. Model C requires fewer and more accessible auxiliaries: local luminary dynamics (for the Sun and Moon), electromagnetic eclipse mechanics, and polar disc navigation. Model D is formally equivalent to A by inversion and has the same auxiliary count. Model B requires the Copernican coordinate equivalence. Model E is unfalsifiable in its strong form (see criterion c).

Criterion (c): Empirical Uniqueness vs. Underdetermination

Models A and D are empirically equivalent under the inversion transformation for all optical observations. Models A and B are empirically equivalent for all geocentrically accessible positional observations. This means that for a very wide class of observations — perhaps the majority of the evidence base — the observations do not discriminate between these models. They are equally supported by the evidence.

Model C diverges from the others in specific domains: the geometry of long-distance navigation, the behavior of celestial bodies at high southern latitudes, and the prediction of what would be found at the ice frontier. These are the domains where genuine discrimination between models is possible — and they are precisely the domains where data access is most restricted.

Criterion (d): Lakatosian Progressive vs. Degenerating Test

Model A (embedded in the ΛCDM framework) has shown a mixed profile: historically progressive (the successful prediction of CMB structure, galaxy formation, etc.), but recently showing signs of degeneration under the Hubble tension, S₈ tension, and dark matter detection failure. Each anomaly has been addressed by protective auxiliary adjustment without generating novel confirmed predictions of comparable scope. By the Lakatosian criterion, Model A's embedding framework is currently in a degenerating phase.

Model C, by contrast, has not had the resources to develop a research programme of comparable scale. Its "Lakatosian score" cannot be evaluated on the same terms.

Criterion (e): Circularity Test

Multiple lines of evidence for Model A fail the circularity test. GPS coordinates presuppose the spheroid model to which they refer. Satellite imagery is processed through algorithms that assume the spheroidal geometry. Nautical charts are constructed on spheroidal projections. The astronaut's view from the International Space Station is interpreted through a framework that assumes the station is orbiting a sphere. None of these constitute independent evidence; they are instances of the model confirming itself.

No final judgment is issued in this section. The task of Part III is comparison, not verdict. The comparisons performed here will be developed in Parts IV through VI.

OBS · 4.1

Physics of Motion: Galilean Frames, Coriolis, Foucault

The Orthodox Reading

On Model A, the Earth rotates about its polar axis with angular velocity ω = 7.292115 × 10⁻⁵ rad s⁻¹ (corresponding to a sidereal period of 23 hours, 56 minutes, 4 seconds). This rotation produces:

(1) The Coriolis effect: an apparent deflection of moving objects in Earth's rotating reference frame, described by the pseudo-force F_C = -2m(ω × v), where v is the velocity of the object in the rotating frame. The deflection is to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere. Observed effects include the rotation of large-scale atmospheric and oceanic circulation patterns, the deflection of long-range ballistic projectiles, and the behavior of Foucault pendulums.

(2) The Foucault pendulum: a long pendulum that appears to rotate its plane of oscillation relative to the Earth's surface. At the North Pole, the plane makes one full rotation in one sidereal day; at latitude φ, the period is T = 24h / sin(φ) hours. The first public demonstration was by Léon Foucault in the Panthéon, Paris, in 1851. The rotation is clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and counterclockwise in the Southern Hemisphere.

The Coriolis deflection of a projectile fired northward at 45° N latitude for a range of 100 km is approximately 300 m to the right. This deflection is directly observed in long-range artillery practice and has been incorporated into ballistic tables since the 19th century.

Tensions in the Orthodox Reading

The attribution of Coriolis effects to Earth's rotation requires the specific dynamic assumptions of Newtonian mechanics in a rotating frame. The Duhem-Quine thesis applies: the observation "the pendulum plane rotates" does not by itself confirm Earth's rotation; it confirms (Earth rotates with angular velocity ω) ∧ (Newtonian mechanics applies to pendulum dynamics) ∧ (no other force is present that could cause the same rotation). Each conjunct is an auxiliary that can, in principle, be challenged.

How Model C Handles This Evidence

On Model C, the flat disc is non-rotating. The Foucault pendulum effect must be explained by an alternative mechanism. The most developed account invokes the stellar frame or aethereal frame: on the hypothesis that the celestial canopy rotates above the disc (the stars rotate about the polar axis), and on the hypothesis that pendulums are coupled to the rotating stellar frame (Machian influence), the pendulum would exhibit apparent rotation relative to the terrestrial surface. Ernst Mach's principle — that inertia is determined by the distribution of mass in the universe — was taken seriously by Einstein and remains an active research topic; it provides at least a theoretical framework within which the Foucault effect could arise without planetary rotation.

Quantitatively, on a disc model with a rotating celestial frame, the Foucault period at latitude φ of the disc would be T_disc = T_stellar / sin(φ_disc), where φ_disc is the angular distance from the pole on the disc. If the disc geometry maps to the spheroidal latitude by a specific projection, the predicted Foucault periods on the disc model and the sphere model can be made to agree. This is an auxiliary hypothesis, but no more problematic than the Newtonian dynamics auxiliary required by Model A.

Model C's treatment of the Coriolis deflection of artillery requires that the deflection arise from the Machian stellar frame rather than Earth's rotation. The numerical agreement can be preserved provided the coupling constant between the stellar frame and inertial motion is correctly calibrated.

OBS · 4.2

Gravity and the Variation of g with Latitude

The Orthodox Reading

On Model A, gravitational acceleration at the Earth's surface varies with latitude according to the Somigliana formula (a geodetically precise refinement of earlier approximations):

\[ g(\varphi) = g_e \frac{1 + k \sin^2\varphi}{\sqrt{1 - e^2 \sin^2\varphi}} \]

where \( g_e = 9.7803253359 \, \text{m s}^{-2} \) is the equatorial value, \( k = (b g_p - a g_e)/(a g_e) \approx 0.00193185 \), and e² ≈ 0.00669438. A simplified version:

\[ g(\varphi) = 9.780327 (1 + 0.0053024 \sin^2\varphi - 0.0000058 \sin^2 2\varphi) \, \text{m s}^{-2} \]

The variation amounts to approximately 0.5% between the equator and the poles. The equatorial value is lower because (a) the equatorial surface is farther from the Earth's center (less gravitational acceleration by inverse square law) and (b) the centrifugal effect of rotation reduces effective gravity at the equator.

The measured variations in g across the Earth's surface, as compiled in international gravity surveys and the WGS84 gravity model, are consistent with this formula at the level of geodetic precision.

Tensions

The formula includes a centrifugal correction that depends on Earth's rotation. If the rotation is questioned (as on Model C), the centrifugal term drops out. On a flat, non-rotating disc, the entire variation in g would need to be explained by variation in the density of the underlying substrate or by altitude variation. This is a genuine challenge for Model C: the observed pole-to-equator variation in g has no obvious mechanism in the disc model other than latitude-dependent substrate density.

How Models B, C, D Handle This

Model B (Tychonic) predicts the same g variation as Model A, since in the Earth-centered frame, the apparent centrifugal effect of the rotating reference frame produces an identical correction.

Model C must either deny the measured variation in g (which would require challenging the gravity survey methodology) or attribute it to variation in local substrate density or topography. The variation in g between, say, the Gulf of Mexico (g ≈ 9.781 m s⁻²) and Scandinavia (g ≈ 9.823 m s⁻²) is real and measured by precision gravimeters. This variation cannot be dismissed; it must be explained. On Model C, the most plausible explanation is variation in substrate density, which is not in principle impossible but requires a specific model of the disc's subsurface structure.

Model D predicts g variation inversely with distance from the shell, which produces the same qualitative pattern as Model A (greater g at the poles, lesser g at the equator) if the equatorial regions are farther from the outer surface (center of the sphere) than the polar regions — but this is geometrically inconsistent with the inversion geometry. Model D faces a genuine difficulty with the g variation.

OBS · 4.3

Optics, Refraction, and the Horizon

The Orthodox Reading

On Model A, the geometric horizon at height h above the Earth's surface is at a distance d ≈ √(2Rh), where R = 6,371 km is the mean radius. For h = 1.8 m (observer eye height), d ≈ 4.8 km. For h = 100 m (observation tower), d ≈ 35.7 km. The "dip of the horizon" — the angle below horizontal at which the geometric horizon lies — is:

\[ \delta = \arccos\left(\frac{R}{R+h}\right) \approx \sqrt{\frac{2h}{R}} \text{ radians} \]

For h = 1.8 m, δ ≈ 0.0275° ≈ 1.65 arcminutes. This is a small but measurable angle.

Atmospheric refraction modifies the effective horizon distance. Under standard atmospheric conditions (temperature lapse rate ≈ 6.5 K/km), the refraction index gradient curves light rays downward with a radius of curvature approximately 7R (i.e., the Earth's radius scaled by a factor of 7). The effective "refraction-corrected" horizon is approximately 7% farther than the geometric horizon.

A Key Tension

The oft-cited observation that "the horizon always rises to eye level" is presented as evidence of flatness. On Model A, the geometric horizon dip is too small to be perceived by the naked eye at ordinary heights (the visual angle resolution of the human eye is approximately 1 arcminute), consistent with the perceived "eye-level horizon." The claimed evidence cuts both ways: both a flat surface and a spheroid of radius 6,371 km predict an effectively horizontal horizon at eye-level heights accessible to unaided human observation.

Photographic and optical evidence of ships disappearing hull-first over the horizon (§ 5.6) constitutes the strongest direct optical argument for Model A, and will be addressed there. For the present, we note that atmospheric refraction — particularly temperature inversions, common over water — can produce optical effects that mimic the hull-first disappearance expected on a spheroid: "looming" (superior mirage) can make objects behind the geometric horizon visible, while "sinking" (inferior mirage) can produce the appearance of hull-first disappearance on a flat surface. The disambiguation between these effects and genuine geometric curvature requires careful measurement of atmospheric conditions that is not routinely performed.

Model C's Treatment

On Model C, the flat surface predicts a horizon that is purely an effect of atmospheric scattering and perspective. As the distance to an object increases, the angular subtended by the object decreases; at a sufficient distance, the object is no longer visually resolved and "disappears" — bottom first if the gradient of atmospheric scattering increases with proximity to the surface (as it does under typical humidity gradients). The flat-disc model predicts that the horizon is at the limit of atmospheric visibility, not a geometrically fixed distance. This prediction is consistent with the observation that the horizon distance varies significantly with atmospheric conditions — more than the geometric model predicts.

OBS · 4.4

Celestial Motion and Stellar Parallax

The Orthodox Reading

If the Earth orbits the Sun at 1 AU, then over six months, the Earth moves approximately 2 AU (roughly 3 × 10¹¹ m) transversely. Nearby stars should therefore appear to shift in position relative to distant background stars by an angle p (the parallax) where tan(p/2) ≈ 1 AU / distance. The nearest star system, α Centauri, has a parallax of 0.742 arcseconds, corresponding to a distance of approximately 1.35 parsecs (4.2 light-years). Stellar parallax was first measured by Friedrich Bessel in 1838 for the star 61 Cygni (Astronomische Nachrichten 16 (1838), col. 65).

The orthodox argument is that stellar parallax unambiguously confirms both Earth's revolution around the Sun and the large stellar distances. On the Neo-Tychonic model (B), the parallax is equally predictable with a geocentric orbit of the stellar sphere. On Model C, the parallax requires further analysis.

Tensions and Model C's Treatment

On Model C, the stars are part of a rotating celestial canopy at finite altitude above the disc. The parallax observations would appear as periodic apparent displacements of nearby stars relative to the background. On the local luminary interpretation, these displacements could arise from the observer's latitude-dependent position on the disc combined with the finite distance of the stellar layer. The quantitative predictions differ from those of the orthodox model at the level of sub-arcsecond precision, which is the level at which stellar parallax is measured.

This constitutes a genuine challenge for Model C: the consistency of parallax measurements across thousands of stars, processed through the Hipparcos and Gaia satellite missions, yields a highly consistent distance scale that is geometrically coherent with the spheroidal model. For Model C to accommodate this, either (a) the stellar distances must be small and the stars at a fixed layer at a specific altitude, with the dispersion of observed parallaxes explained by something other than actual distance variation; or (b) the Gaia data must be interpreted within a disc model, which requires a non-trivial transformation.

This is one of the domains in which Model A has a genuine evidential advantage over Model C by criterion (c) of the evaluation framework.

OBS · 4.5

Eclipse Mechanics

The Orthodox Reading

On Model A, a lunar eclipse occurs when the Earth's shadow falls on the Moon. The shadow is the projection of the spherical Earth by the Sun, yielding an umbral cone of circular cross-section. The boundary of the umbra as it crosses the Moon is visibly curved — a circular arc — regardless of the orientation of the Earth at the time of the eclipse. Aristotle recorded this observation (On the Heavens, Book II, ch. 14) as evidence that the Earth is spherical.

The argument from eclipse shadow shape is one of the oldest and strongest in the catalogue of evidence for Model A. For the shadow to be circular at all orientations, the shadow-casting body must itself be spherical (or more precisely, must project a circular shadow cross-section in all orientations, which for an opaque body requires spherical geometry).

Tension: What Is the Shadow-Casting Body?

The eclipse argument establishes that something is casting a circular shadow on the Moon. The attribution of that shadow to the Earth requires the additional assumption that the Earth lies between the Sun and Moon during lunar eclipse — an inference that follows from the orbital model but is not directly observable. If the shadow-casting body were something else — a hypothetical celestial body (the counter-Earth of Philolaus; or on Model C, a "shadow object" or dark luminary) — the eclipse observation would not confirm Earth's sphericity.

Proponents of Model C have proposed, in various formulations, that lunar eclipses are caused by a "shadow body" — a dark object distinct from the Earth — that passes between the Sun and Moon. This proposal is testable in principle: if the shadow-casting body is the Earth, the eclipse's progress across the Moon should be consistent with the Earth's orbital position as independently determined by triangulation. The consistency is apparently achieved in the orthodox model. Model C's shadow-body proposal is an ad hoc auxiliary (it generates no independent predictions) and must be acknowledged as such; it survives the criticism only by criterion (b) — the auxiliary requirement — not by generating independent confirmatory evidence.

Model D's Identical Problem

Model D, the concave earth, has an additional difficulty: on the inside of a sphere, the Earth's shadow would project inward, not outward, and would never fall on any celestial body orbiting outside the shell. Eclipse mechanics are therefore incompatible with Model D without major additional auxiliary hypotheses about the nature and position of the Moon and Sun relative to the shell.

OBS · 4.6

Geography, Long-Distance Navigation, and Circumnavigation

The Orthodox Reading

Ferdinand Magellan's circumnavigation (1519–1522) is typically cited as the first empirical circumnavigation of the Earth. Subsequent circumnavigations, including solo circumnavigations and continuous tracking by GPS, provide apparently direct evidence that returning to a starting point by traveling in one direction is possible — a feature of closed, spheroidal surfaces.

Long-distance aviation routes (great-circle routes) are planned using spheroidal geometry. The claimed agreement between predicted and actual flight times, fuel consumption, and waypoints is cited as evidence for Model A.

Tensions

Two navigational facts are regularly cited as tensions within the orthodox model's prediction:

1. The anomalous transpolar route. Great-circle routes between cities in the southern hemisphere — Sydney to Buenos Aires, Johannesburg to Santiago de Chile — on a sphere should pass over Antarctica. On the standard spheroid, Sydney (34°S, 151°E) to Santiago (33°S, 71°W) has a great-circle route that crosses approximately 60°S latitude, passing near the Antarctic peninsula. The commercially operated flight (LATAM Airlines, currently the primary operator) does not in fact fly over Antarctica; it routes via North America or directly across the Pacific at mid-latitudes. The great-circle distance is approximately 11,300 km; the actual flight routes are longer.

The orthodox explanation attributes this to the paucity of emergency landing sites and technical restrictions on ETOPS routes over unpopulated ocean regions, combined with the polar jet stream.

On Model C, the azimuthal equidistant projection (centered on the North Pole) predicts that routes between southern hemisphere cities should be approximately circular arcs that do not pass near the South Pole (which, on the disc model, corresponds to the interior of the ice frontier circle). The actual commercial route patterns are, as noted in § 6.6, broadly consistent with predictions from the azimuthal equidistant projection.

2. Circumnavigation geometry. On a disc model, circumnavigation of the North Pole is possible: a traveler proceeding in a circle around the pole returns to their starting point. However, a traveler proceeding in a southerly direction and claiming to circumnavigate the globe by crossing the equator and continuing south would, on Model C, never return to the starting point if the surface is truly flat and infinite — or would traverse the full disc radius if the surface is bounded. The claimed circumnavigations by Magellan and his successors are consistent with both a spheroid and a sufficiently large disc; the disambiguation requires measurements of the angular extent of the traversed path, which depend on the geometry being assumed.

OBS · 4.7

Tides and Lunar Gravitation

The Orthodox Reading

On Model A, tides are primarily caused by the differential gravitational attraction of the Moon (and to a lesser extent the Sun) across the diameter of the Earth. The tidal force at a point on the Earth's surface is approximately:

\[ F_{tidal} \approx \frac{2GM_{Moon} R_{Earth}}{d^3_{Moon}} \approx 1.1 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{m s}^{-2} \]

where R_Earth = 6.371 × 10⁶ m, M_Moon = 7.342 × 10²² kg, and d_Moon = 3.844 × 10⁸ m. This produces the familiar pattern of two tidal bulges on the Earth (one toward the Moon, one away), resulting in two high tides and two low tides per (approximately) 24 hours, with a period governed by the lunar orbital period (M2 tidal constituent period: 12.42 hours).

The predictions of tidal theory are highly quantitative and in good agreement with observation over most ocean basins.

Tensions

Tidal behavior near coastlines and in enclosed basins (bays, estuaries) deviates substantially from the simple two-bulge model and requires elaborate localized corrections (resonance effects, Kelvin waves, amphidromic points). The tidal patterns at specific locations are predicted by numerical simulation rather than derived from the basic gravitational formula. These simulations presuppose the spheroidal Earth model, and their agreement with observation confirms the combination of the tide-generating mechanism and the spheroidal geometry, not either independently.

Model C's Treatment

On Model C, the Moon is a local luminary and the gravitational/tidal mechanism must be replaced by an electromagnetic or pressure-wave mechanism. The periodic tidal signal (dominantly semi-diurnal, approximately 12.42-hour period) must be explained by the periodic overhead passage of the Moon's spiral path. This is qualitatively possible but requires that the Moon's electromagnetic or pressure influence be much stronger than the orthodox model proposes at the small effective distances of the local luminary. The quantitative predictions of Model C's tidal mechanism have not been developed to the level of the orthodox tidal model, which constitutes a genuine deficiency in the developed state of Model C.

Pars Quinta

RESPONSES TO THE STRONGEST OBJECTIONS

The format of medieval disputation is adopted: Objectio states the objection at its full strength; Sed Contra introduces the counter-consideration; Respondeo develops the full response.

Quaestio 1

Objectio: "Satellite Imagery Directly Shows the Earth is Round"

Objectio

Photographs taken from low Earth orbit (NASA, ESA, JAXA, private operators including SpaceX and Planet Labs) show the Earth as a sphere. The curvature is visible at altitudes above approximately 10 km; the full disc is visible from geostationary orbit (35,786 km) and beyond. The imagery is consistent across agencies, including agencies of nations historically antagonistic to the United States. The existence of this imagery constitutes direct visual evidence that the Earth is spheroid.

Sed Contra

The evidential weight of satellite imagery depends on the following auxiliaries being independently verified: (a) the satellites are where they are claimed to be; (b) the cameras on the satellites record what is physically before them without systematic distortion; (c) the imagery is not composited, processed, or assembled from multiple sources in ways that could introduce artifacts; (d) the physical cameras are operating under conditions consistent with their specifications (vacuum, radiation environment, extreme temperature cycling).

Respondeo

No single piece of satellite imagery constitutes direct observation in Husserl's phenomenological sense; all of it is instrument-mediated data of type I. Each piece of imagery is a product of: signal acquisition by a sensor array; analog-to-digital conversion; transmission to ground stations through a computational protocol; decompression and processing by ground-based software; and display on a monitor. Each step in this chain involves assumptions that can be challenged.

More specifically: virtually every widely circulated "whole Earth" photograph is composite or enhanced. The iconic "Blue Marble" image (NASA, December 7, 1972, Apollo 17) is frequently cited as a direct photograph; it is in fact taken from approximately 45,000 km altitude and shows the Earth at a spatial resolution insufficient to resolve sub-continental features. All subsequent "whole Earth" images — from the DSCOVR satellite's EPIC camera, from geostationary meteorological satellites — are produced by combining multiple narrow-field sensor arrays operating in different spectral bands and assembled into a composite image by software. NASA explicitly states on its website that such images are composites.

The use of wide-angle lenses introduces barrel or pincushion distortion that systematically curves any straight line; such distortion can make a flat surface appear curved. Independent photographic analysis of images taken from high-altitude balloons (reaching approximately 30–40 km) often shows apparent curvature that is demonstrably lens-induced, which can be eliminated by using rectilinear lenses.

On Model C, a flat surface photographed from very high altitude with a wide-angle lens could appear curved at the edges due to lens geometry, and the atmospheric gradient visible at the horizon would be circular (as the atmosphere's edge is circular when viewed from above, whether or not the surface is flat, since the atmosphere is bounded by gravity perpendicular to the surface). The "blue disc" visible from geostationary altitude is consistent with either a spheroid or a disc, since both present a circular bounded region when viewed from high altitude.

The auxiliary required to convert satellite imagery into proof of sphericity — that the imagery accurately records geometrically undistorted reality — has not been independently verified at the level of precision required. The imagery is therefore evidence of type I with a substantial auxiliary cost, not direct observational evidence of type D.

Quaestio 2

Objectio: "GPS Works"

Objectio

The Global Positioning System (GPS) operates by computing the receiver's position from the time-of-flight of signals from at least four satellites. The positions of the satellites are computed using orbital mechanics that depend on the Earth being a spheroid. The system works: it routinely delivers position accuracy of meters or better. If the underlying spheroidal geometry were incorrect, GPS would fail. It does not fail; therefore the spheroidal geometry is correct.

Sed Contra

The argument presupposes that the only way to produce a functioning GPS-like system is to use the spheroidal model. This is not demonstrated. A mathematical framework that correctly predicts signal time-of-flights and yields internally consistent position coordinates would function equally well regardless of whether the underlying geometry corresponds to a spheroid or to any other surface, provided the mathematical transformation between the coordinate system used and the physical positions is internally consistent.

Respondeo

GPS delivers coordinates in the WGS84 coordinate system. The WGS84 coordinate system is a mathematical abstraction — a set of three real numbers — that is assigned to physical locations by a convention. The system works, in the sense that the coordinates it assigns are internally consistent: if the system assigns coordinates (x₁, y₁, z₁) to New York and (x₂, y₂, z₂) to London, then the distance formula applied to these coordinates gives a number that, when converted to travel time by aircraft or ship at known velocities, yields consistent predictions.

But "works" in this sense does not entail "gives the correct shape of the Earth." It entails only that the WGS84 coordinate system is an accurate representation of the topology of the observable surface — i.e., that its metric is correctly calibrated for the ranges of distance accessible to measurement. The topology of the observable surface is consistent with both a spheroid and a flat disc of the same characteristic dimension, as long as the edge (ice frontier on Model C) is either inaccessible or mapped onto coordinates that are never accessed in ordinary GPS use.

The deeper point is that GPS coordinates are not positions on a sphere; they are outputs of a computational model. The model is calibrated to deliver consistent results, and it does so. The inference from "the model delivers consistent results" to "the model's geometric premises are literally true" is the inference from empirical adequacy to truth that van Fraassen (The Scientific Image, p. 69) identifies as unjustified.

A satellite navigation system designed around a flat-disc model with the same characteristic distances, with orbital mechanics computed in a geocentric non-rotating frame, could in principle deliver the same positional outputs over the accessible surface. The difference between the models would manifest only at the edges of the disc — in the Antarctic region — which GPS does not normally cover with full-constellation accuracy and which independent verification is difficult to arrange.

Quaestio 3

Objectio: "Commercial Aviation and Circumnavigation Prove the Sphere"

Objectio

Commercial airlines routinely fly great-circle routes between any two cities on Earth and arrive with predicted fuel consumption and travel times. The routes are calculated using spheroidal geometry. Aircraft equipped with inertial navigation systems accumulate attitude and heading changes consistent with spheroidal geometry over long flights. Solo circumnavigators (by air and sea) return to their starting points having traveled in approximately one direction. These facts require a closed, spheroidal surface.

Sed Contra

As noted in § 4.6, the claimed flight routes over southern hemisphere city pairs do not in practice use the great-circle routes predicted by the spheroidal model; they deviate to avoid Antarctica, with the deviation attributed to emergency landing constraints and wind patterns. The disc model predicts substantially different great-circle paths for many transpolar routes, and the observable route patterns are more consistent with one than with the other.

Respondeo

The circumnavigation argument requires careful examination. A traveler who departs from a location, travels in a nominally constant eastward direction, and returns to the starting point has demonstrated one of two things: (a) the surface is a closed manifold (e.g., a sphere), or (b) the traveler's path was not actually straight but followed a closed curve on a non-spherical surface. On the disc model, an eastward circumnavigation of the North Pole is a closed circle around the pole; the traveler follows a curve of constant latitude (on the disc, a circle of constant radius from the center) and returns to the starting point without having traveled over the ice frontier.

Magellan's original circumnavigation followed a path consistent with the polar circle interpretation: the route did not go "over" either pole; it circled around the Earth's rotational axis, which on Model C is the pole of the disc. The fact of returning to the starting point is equally consistent with a closed curved path on a flat disc as with a great circle on a sphere.

Inertial navigation systems accumulate rotation of the platform relative to its initial orientation through gyroscopes. On a spheroid, a vehicle traveling along a great circle returns to its starting orientation; on a flat disc, a vehicle traveling along a circle of latitude does not return to the same orientation (there is a net rotation equal to the solid angle enclosed by the path). This constitutes a genuine discriminating test, and it is one of the stronger arguments for Model A. However, the test requires precise knowledge of the accumulated attitude change over a full circumnavigation, which is affected by gyroscope drift, corrections made during flight, and the impossibility of distinguishing inertial from non-inertial effects in the relevant coordinate transformation.

On the question of great-circle routes and flight times: it is significant that no commercial airline routinely operates scheduled service from Sydney to Buenos Aires via Antarctica — a direct connection that would be 30-40% shorter in distance than any currently operating route on the spheroidal geometry, and for which there is no comparable antipodal shortcut on the disc geometry. The absence of this route is attributed by orthodox cartography to the regulatory, meteorological, and emergency-landing constraints of polar flying. This explanation may be correct. But it remains the case that the route most discriminating between the two geometric models — the transpolar southern hemisphere connection — is precisely the route that is not flown.

Quaestio 4

Objectio: "Every Space Agency, Including Adversarial Ones, Confirms"

Objectio

The consensus on Earth's spheroidal shape is held not only by NASA but by ESA (European Space Agency), JAXA (Japan), Roscosmos (Russia), CNSA (China), and ISRO (India). Nations with strong geopolitical rivalries and different interests have independently arrived at the same conclusion. If the spheroid were wrong, at least one adversarial agency would have an incentive to expose the discrepancy. The unanimity of adversarial consensus is powerful evidence.

Sed Contra

The argument assumes that each agency has genuinely independently verified the Earth's shape from first principles, rather than having adopted the WGS84 and other international standards as a shared coordinate convention. The argument also assumes that the incentive structure is correctly described: that agencies would expose a geometric discrepancy if they found one. Neither assumption is self-evident.

Respondeo

The world's space agencies do not operate on entirely independent data processing systems. The global reference frames used in geodesy — the International Terrestrial Reference Frame (ITRF), the Conventional Terrestrial Reference System (CTRS) — are maintained by international bodies (IERS, BIPM) with extensive collaboration between agencies. A space agency that adopts ITRF as its reference frame is not independently verifying the Earth's shape; it is adopting a coordinate convention that was established by a collaborative body that includes all major space-faring nations.

The deeper issue is that the independent verification of a geometric claim requires independent methodology — not independent use of the same coordinate framework. Each space agency uses the same orbital mechanics (Newtonian + GR corrections), the same tracking networks (DSN, ESTRACK), and the same reference frame standards. Their "agreement" is agreement in using the same conventions, not independent convergence on the same truth.

On the question of incentive: the geopolitical incentive structure of space agencies is not to expose discrepancies in the shared reference frame but to maintain the functioning of the shared infrastructure (GPS, satellite communications, weather forecasting) that depends on that reference frame. If an anomaly were discovered in the geodetic framework, the incentive would be to understand and correct it within the framework, not to announce that the Earth's shape had been misunderstood.

The argument from adversarial consensus is the argument from authority applied at global scale. By criterion (a) of the evaluation framework (§ 1.6), it is evidence of type C (institutional consensus) — the weakest epistemic modality — and must be discounted accordingly.

Quaestio 5

Objectio: "The Earth's Shadow on the Moon During Lunar Eclipse Is Circular"

Objectio

During a lunar eclipse, the shadow of the Earth projected on the Moon is visibly a circular arc at every orientation. Since a sphere is the only convex body that projects a circular shadow in all orientations, the Earth must be spherical (Aristotle, On the Heavens, II.14).

Sed Contra

Aristotle's argument, while elegant, has two gaps. First, it proves the Earth spherical only if the Earth is the body casting the shadow on the Moon. Second, it proves the Earth spherical if the shadow's boundary is a circular arc in all orientations — which requires that the shadow-casting body project a circular cross-section in all orientations.

Respondeo

The body casting the shadow during a lunar eclipse is identified as the Earth by inference from the heliocentric orbital model. The inference goes: the Moon is seen to enter the shadow from the east (in the direction from which the Sun is located); the shadow's position relative to the Sun is where the Earth's shadow should be if the Earth orbits the Sun with the Moon at greater distance. This identification requires the heliocentric orbital model as an auxiliary. In the geocentric model (B or C), the shadow is cast by a body that lies between the Sun and the Moon; if the Earth is flat and below the Moon, the Earth's flat disc projects a shadow with elliptical cross-section at most orientations, not a circular one.

This is a genuine challenge for Model C. On the flat disc model, if the Earth casts the lunar eclipse shadow, the shadow projected at oblique angles would be elliptical, not circular. The Model C response requires either: (a) the shadow body is not the Earth but a distinct dark celestial body (shadow object), which is an ad hoc auxiliary with no independent support; or (b) the eclipse occurs through a mechanism that does not involve shadow-casting (e.g., the Moon enters a region of space where it loses its luminosity due to the proximity of some force field, and the circular boundary is an artifact of that force field's symmetry). Option (b) is less ad hoc than (a) if the force field in question has independent theoretical motivation, which at present it does not.

The Aristotelian eclipse argument is, in this monograph's assessment, the single strongest observational argument for Model A's spherical geometry. It does not depend on instruments; it is a direct geometrical observation (the shape of the shadow boundary). The Model C response to it is the weakest response in the whole repertoire. This must be conceded honestly: the eclipse shadow is a genuine evidential challenge that Model C must address more satisfactorily than its current proponents have achieved.

Quaestio 6

Objectio: "Ships Disappear Hull-First Over the Horizon"

Objectio

A ship receding from an observer disappears progressively from the bottom up: the hull vanishes first, then the lower rigging, then the upper masts. This is the direct observation predicted by spheroidal geometry: the lower parts of the ship drop below the horizon (which curves away from the observer) before the upper parts. The effect is restored when a telescope is raised in elevation, confirming the geometric interpretation.

Sed Contra

This observation is also consistent with atmospheric refraction effects that produce progressive bottom-up apparent disappearance on a flat surface. The discrimination between geometric curvature and atmospheric refraction requires measurement of atmospheric conditions not typically performed by casual observers.

Respondeo

The hull-first disappearance is a classic piece of evidence for Model A, cited since antiquity. On a spheroid of radius R, an object of height H disappearing below the geometric horizon at distance d_horizon will have its base obscured when:

\[ d_{base} = \sqrt{2R \cdot h_{eye}} \]

and its top visible until:

\[ d_{top} = \sqrt{2R \cdot (h_{eye} + H)} \]

For a ship of height H = 20 m and an observer eye height h_eye = 1.8 m, the hull disappears at d ≈ 4.8 km and the top of the mast at d ≈ 16.7 km. The progressive disappearance over this 12 km range is geometrically predicted and is in principle quantitatively testable.

However, as the Bedford Level experiment (§ 6.3) and subsequent analyses demonstrate, atmospheric refraction is a systematic complicating factor over water. A temperature inversion — in which air temperature increases with altitude above the water surface, as commonly occurs over warm water — changes the effective refractive index gradient and curves light rays. Under a strong inversion, light rays curve downward with a radius of curvature much less than R, effectively creating an apparent horizon much closer to the surface. This can produce the visual effect of hull-first disappearance on a flat surface.

The standard response is that the hull-first effect is reversible by raising the telescope to a higher position, which would not be possible under refraction alone. This response is partially correct: a genuine geometric disappearance beyond a curved horizon cannot be reversed by raising the observer's elevation, while a refraction-induced disappearance can. But the raising-telescope test is not decisive: a temperature inversion strong enough to curve light all the way to the surface will produce an apparent horizon at a fixed angle below horizontal; raising the observer's elevation (while remaining within the inversion layer) will recover lower parts of the ship because the observer is now seeing around the curved light path from a different vantage point within the gradient.

The disambiguation requires a quantitative theodolite measurement of the exact angle at which each part of the ship disappears, combined with simultaneous atmospheric profiling (temperature, humidity, lapse rate). Such measurements were performed by Wallace at the Bedford Level (§ 6.3) with carefully controlled methodology; his results confirmed the spheroidal prediction. However, the Bedford Level result is contested (see § 6.3), and the question of whether Wallace's experimental setup adequately controlled for refraction is the central point of dispute.

Net assessment: the hull-first disappearance observation is genuine evidence for Model A but not conclusive, because its evidential force depends on the atmospheric model as an auxiliary. It requires criterion (b) — auxiliary counting — to be evaluated, and the auxiliary is large and uncertain.

Quaestio 7

Objectio: "Foucault's Pendulum Demonstrates Earth's Rotation"

Objectio

Foucault's pendulum, placed at the North Pole, would rotate its plane of oscillation by 360° in 24 hours. At latitude φ, it rotates at 360° × sin(φ) per 24 hours. This precise latitude-dependent behavior is observed in installations worldwide (the Panthéon in Paris, the Smithsonian Institution in Washington, the UN headquarters in New York) and is directly predicted by Earth's rotation at the Newtonian mechanics level. The latitude dependence is a quantitative test: it cannot arise by coincidence.

Sed Contra

As noted in § 4.1, the Foucault observation is a test of the conjunction (Earth's rotation) ∧ (Newtonian dynamics of pendulum) ∧ (no other rotational coupling). The latitude-dependent formula is equally derivable from a geocentric model in which the stellar sphere rotates about the polar axis, with a Machian coupling between the stellar frame and the pendulum's reference frame.

Respondeo

Mach's principle — that local inertial frames are determined by the global distribution of matter — was considered by Einstein in the development of general relativity and partially motivated the form of GR, though GR does not fully implement Mach's principle. In a fully Machian theory, the rotation of the stellar sphere would completely determine local inertial frames, and a Foucault pendulum would rotate relative to the Earth's surface even if the Earth were stationary.

The quantitative test of the latitude dependence — T = 24h / sin(φ) — is satisfied equally well on the stellar rotation interpretation: the period of a Foucault pendulum in a Machian stellar frame rotating at angular velocity Ω is T = 2π / (Ω sin φ). If Ω corresponds to one sidereal rotation per day, the formula is identical to the one derived from Earth's rotation. The two interpretations (rotating Earth, stationary stellar frame) and (stationary Earth, rotating stellar frame) are physically distinguishable only by independent determination of which is the inertial frame — which is precisely what is at issue.

The Sagnac effect provides an additional test: the Sagnac effect (a phase difference between counter-propagating beams of light in a rotating reference frame) is observed in ring-laser gyroscopes and is consistent with Earth's rotation at the expected rate. But again, on a Machian interpretation, the rotating stellar frame produces an identical Sagnac signal. The two hypotheses are experimentally degenerate for all Earth-based experiments.

The discriminating test would require a Foucault pendulum or ring laser at a location where the stellar frame and the Earth-rotation prediction diverge — which they do not for any Earth-based location, by construction.

Quaestio 8

Objectio: "Stars Rotate About Different Celestial Poles in Northern and Southern Hemispheres"

Objectio

In the Northern Hemisphere, stars appear to circle the North Celestial Pole (marked approximately by Polaris). In the Southern Hemisphere, stars appear to circle the South Celestial Pole (in the constellation Crux/Musca, near Sigma Octantis). On a flat disc model with a single stellar canopy rotating about the North Pole, stars should appear to rotate about a single axis from any point on the disc; there should be no "South Celestial Pole." The existence of a southern celestial pole is evidence for a spheroidal geometry.

Sed Contra

On the disc model, observers near the outer edge of the disc (the high southern latitudes of the conventional map) look up at a celestial canopy from a vantage point that is closer to the edge than to the center. The geometry of viewing a rotating disc canopy from near its edge would produce an apparent "rotational center" that appears shifted toward the horizon.

Respondeo

On Model C's disc geometry, an observer at the equator looks up at a stellar canopy rotating about the pole. The pole appears overhead. As the observer moves toward the outer edge of the disc, the North Pole appears increasingly close to the horizon (consistent with the observation that Polaris is at low elevation in the Southern Hemisphere). At a sufficient distance from the center, the observer would see the stellar canopy rotating about a point at or near the horizon — which would appear as a "southern celestial pole" near the horizon, in the direction of the disc edge.

The quantitative geometry of this effect is as follows: on a disc of radius R_disc, an observer at distance r from the center looks up at a canopy rotating about the center at altitude h_canopy above the disc. The apparent angular elevation of the rotation axis is:

\[ \alpha = \arctan\left(\frac{h_{canopy}}{r}\right) \]

For r = 0 (North Pole), α = 90° (pole directly overhead, consistent with observation). For r = R_equator (equatorial region), α = arctan(h_canopy / R_equator). For this to equal approximately 0° (pole on the horizon, as observed near the South Pole of the orthodox map, which corresponds to the outer edge of the disc), we need h_canopy / r → 0, i.e., the observer is near the edge of the disc.

The predicted position of the apparent "south celestial pole" on Model C would depend on the disc radius and canopy altitude. At intermediate "southern" latitudes (observers at distances from center roughly 60–70% of the disc radius), the apparent pole would appear at intermediate elevation angles — consistent with observations at those latitudes that place the South Celestial Pole at an angular elevation equal to the southern latitude. This quantitative matching is non-trivial and represents one of Model C's genuine predictive achievements: the apparent dual-polar structure of the sky is reproduced by the disc model without requiring a second physical pole.

The Antarctic Restriction Regime as Circumstantial Evidence

The Antarctic Treaty System

The Antarctic Treaty was signed on December 1, 1959, and entered into force on June 23, 1961. Its original twelve signatories include the United States, the Soviet Union, the United Kingdom, France, Australia, Argentina, Chile, and Norway — nations with every reason for geopolitical competition in the Antarctic region. The Treaty's primary provisions: Antarctica is reserved for peaceful purposes; military activities are prohibited; scientific exchange is encouraged; territorial claims are frozen.

Access to Antarctica requires: (a) approval from a Treaty signatory government; (b) coordination with the Scientific Committee on Antarctic Research (SCAR); (c) for any significant expedition, logistical support from national programs (NSF, BAS, AARI, etc.) that control the only bases and transport infrastructure in the region. An independent investigator wishing to survey the southernmost regions by their own means has no established pathway to do so. The cost of mounting a fully independent Antarctic expedition — with independent navigation, independent satellite tracking, and independent geographic measurement — exceeds the resources of any private individual and most non-governmental organizations.

The Michelson-Morley-Miller Residual

Background: The 1887 Michelson-Morley Experiment

Albert Michelson and Edward Morley's 1887 interferometry experiment at the Case School of Applied Science in Cleveland (American Journal of Science 34 (1887), pp. 333–345) is universally cited as having produced a "null result" for the aether — a result interpreted as proving that the Earth moves through no luminiferous medium, and subsequently as confirming Special Relativity.

The experiment measured the fringe shift in an interferometer as the Earth's surface rotated: if the Earth moves through a stationary aether, a fringe shift proportional to v²/c² should be observed (where v is the Earth's orbital velocity, approximately 30 km/s). The expected shift for their apparatus was approximately 0.4 fringes; they observed approximately 0.01 fringes — far below the expected value.

This result is correctly described as "much smaller than expected." It is not correctly described as "null" or "zero." Michelson himself acknowledged a small but systematic signal. The history of a "null result" is a post-hoc simplification.

Dayton Miller's 200,000-Reading Programme

Dayton Miller (1866–1941) was a physicist at Case Western Reserve University who performed the most extensive interferometry experiments ever conducted for aether detection. His work ran from 1902 to 1926, culminating in a comprehensive analysis published as "The Ether-Drift Experiment and the Determination of the Absolute Motion of the Earth" (Reviews of Modern Physics 5 (1933), pp. 203–242).

Miller's experimental programme encompassed:

  • Over 200,000 individual fringe-shift readings
  • Experiments at multiple altitudes (basement level at Case; Mount Wilson Observatory at 1742 m elevation)
  • Experiments in open-air structures (canvas-walled enclosures at Mount Wilson) designed to eliminate thermal shielding effects
  • Systematic variation of azimuth, time of day, and season

Miller's results showed a systematic, non-zero fringe shift with the following properties:

  • Amplitude of approximately 0.10–0.13 fringes (compared to the 0.40 expected for a stationary aether and the ~0.01 observed by Michelson-Morley in the basement)
  • Sidereal periodicity: the phase and amplitude of the observed shift varied with sidereal time (the time determined by the stars, not by the Sun), with a period of one sidereal day (23 hours 56 minutes)
  • Seasonal variation: the amplitude was higher at the equinoxes than at the solstices
  • The apparent motion pointed toward an apex in the Southern Celestial Hemisphere, near the constellation Dorado (right ascension ≈ 4h 54m, declination ≈ −70° 33'), with an apparent velocity of approximately 208 km/s

Miller argued that these systematic effects were real and reflected an actual aether drift — not a stationary absolute-frame aether, but a partially entrained aether in which the Earth's motion through the aether medium produces a signal diminished by altitude-dependent entrainment.

The Shankland 1955 Analysis and Its Limits

A 1955 re-analysis by Robert Shankland and colleagues (Reviews of Modern Physics 27 (1955), pp. 167–178) concluded that Miller's results were due to thermal artifacts — differential temperature changes in the two arms of the interferometer caused by imperfect temperature control in the canvas enclosures. Shankland's analysis showed that if the fringe shifts were plotted against local time (rather than sidereal time), the amplitude and phase patterns could be accounted for by temperature effects of a known form.

However, Shankland's thermal account does not fully address the sidereal periodicity in Miller's data. A thermal artifact correlated with the Sun's position would produce a solar-day (24-hour) periodicity; the sidereal periodicity (23h 56m) differs from the solar day by 4 minutes — a difference that would accumulate to a full cycle of desynchronization over about six months. Miller's data shows sidereal, not solar, periodicity, which is not consistent with a simple thermal explanation.

The physicist John Bell, in "How to Teach Special Relativity" (Progress in Scientific Culture 1 (1976), pp. 1–14; reprinted in Bell, Speakable and Unspeakable in Quantum Mechanics, Cambridge University Press, 1987), noted that Miller's data, taken at face value, is consistent with a Lorentzian aether interpretation — a version of special relativity in which a preferred frame exists but is undetectable except through second-order effects. Bell argued that the Lorentzian interpretation is empirically equivalent to Einsteinian special relativity but preserves the concept of a preferred frame.

The empirical equivalence of Lorentzian aether theory and special relativity was demonstrated by Hendrik Lorentz himself; it means that no experiment within special relativity's domain can distinguish the two. Miller's residual, on the Lorentzian interpretation, is a genuine aether signal; on the Einsteinian interpretation, it is a thermal artifact. The two interpretations cannot be straightforwardly discriminated by the data alone.

Relevance to Model C

On Model A, the Earth orbits the Sun, which implies a large velocity of the Earth relative to the stellar frame (≈ 30 km/s orbital + ≈ 370 km/s cosmological). The Michelson-Morley null result (on the standard reading) is explained by special relativity, which denies the existence of a preferred frame.

On Model C (or any model in which the Earth's surface is stationary), the expected aether drift signal is zero (no orbital motion) or small (only the drift of the solar system relative to the cosmological frame). Miller's observed signal, if genuine, is more consistent with a partially entrained aether in a frame where the Earth's surface is roughly stationary — precisely the prediction of Model C and of the Tychonic Model B. The observed signal of approximately 10 km/s (the surface value, after accounting for altitude-dependent entrainment) would correspond to the solar system's motion relative to the aether, with the Earth's surface not contributing an additional orbital velocity because the Earth is not orbiting.

Miller's results, taken seriously as data rather than dismissed as artifact, therefore constitute circumstantial evidence for a model in which the Earth is not in orbital motion — compatible with both the Tychonic model and with Model C.

Horizon Geometry and the Flat Horizon

The Bedford Level Experiment: Primary Source

Samuel Rowbotham (writing under the pseudonym "Parallax") conducted the first systematic observational study of long-baseline water surface curvature at the Old Bedford River in the Cambridgeshire Fens. His results are reported in Zetetic Astronomy: Earth Not a Globe (London: John B. Day, 1865; 3rd ed., 1881). Rowbotham placed a telescope 8 inches above the water surface and observed a boat with a flag at 2, 4, and 6 miles distance. On the spheroidal model, the curvature at 6 miles should produce a drop of approximately 6 feet below the line of sight; the flag should have been invisible at a telescope height of 8 inches. Rowbotham reported that the flag remained visible at 6 miles.

The 1870 Wallace experiment (§ 4.3) is taken as the definitive refutation of Rowbotham. Wallace used a 13.5-foot observation height rather than 8 inches, set a reference post midway at the same height, and observed that the midpoint post appeared above the line connecting the two endpoints — consistent with the spheroidal model.

The critical objection to Rowbotham's original experiment — which Wallace himself made — is that at 8 inches above water, the telescope is within the atmospheric boundary layer where temperature gradient effects are maximal, and where anomalous refraction is most likely to produce apparent curvature compensation. Rowbotham's result may reflect a real optical effect: at sufficiently low observation height over warm water, the refraction gradient is large enough to produce approximate "lifting" of distant objects to apparent eye level.

The Quantitative Claim

The standard curvature formula for a sphere of radius R = 6,371 km gives a drop of:

\[ d_{drop}(x) \approx \frac{x^2}{2R} \]

For x = 1 km, d_drop ≈ 7.8 cm. For x = 10 km, d_drop ≈ 7.8 m. For x = 100 km (the distance over which the Erie Canal, Suez Canal, and various surveying baselines operate), d_drop ≈ 785 m.

These numbers are large. The Suez Canal, at 193 km length, should show a curvature drop of approximately 2.9 km along its length on the spheroid model. No such drop is accommodated; the canal is at sea level throughout, maintained by a series of hydraulic cuts. The standard response is that the canal follows the curvature of the Earth: the water surface does not "drop" relative to the sea level surface, because sea level is itself curved. This is correct if the Earth is a sphere; the sea level surface is an equipotential surface that, on a spheroid, is curved by the same radius. On a flat disc, sea level is flat, and the canal that follows sea level is flat.

The argument is circular in the sense identified by criterion (e): the observation that the canal "follows the curvature" presupposes that sea level is curved, which presupposes the spheroidal model. It does not provide independent evidence.

Modern Long-Baseline Observations

High-powered telephoto photography over water at distances of 40–80 km, conducted by a number of independent photographers (principally over the Adriatic, the Baltic, and the Great Lakes), has produced a substantial number of images showing distant objects — bridges, towers, islands — at distances well beyond the calculated horizon, at apparent heights consistent with a flat or nearly flat surface, and inconsistent with the expected curvature drop.

The standard response to each such observation is atmospheric refraction. Given the power of anomalous refraction under temperature-inversion conditions, the response is technically capable of accounting for the observation. But by criterion (b), the auxiliary required (an anomalous refraction condition calibrated specifically to match the observation) is large and untested in the specific case. The invocation of anomalous refraction as a blanket explanation for every long-baseline anomaly is, by Lakatos's criterion, a protective modification: it is not independently testable and generates no novel predictions.

The Behavior of the Sun

Angular Diameter Variation

The Sun's angular diameter as observed from the Earth's surface varies between approximately 31.6 arcminutes (at aphelion, approximately July 4) and 32.7 arcminutes (at perihelion, approximately January 3), a variation of approximately 3.3%. On the spheroidal model, this variation is attributed to Earth's elliptical orbit (eccentricity e = 0.0167), which brings the Earth approximately 3.1% closer to the Sun at perihelion than at aphelion.

On Model C, the Sun is a local luminary moving on a spiral path above the disc at a finite altitude h_sun. As the Sun spirals outward from the polar region toward the tropical circle (the "tropic of Cancer" equivalent on the disc), its altitude above the disc surface remains approximately constant (if it maintains a fixed height above the surface), but its distance from any given observer on the disc varies. An observer at the disc's center sees the Sun overhead in summer and at lower altitude in winter; an observer at the equatorial region sees more dramatic angular changes.

The observed annual variation in solar angular diameter (3.3%) is consistent with a variation in Sun-Earth distance of approximately 3.3% (since angular diameter scales as 1/distance for a fixed-size object). On the spheroidal model, this distance variation corresponds to orbital eccentricity. On Model C, it would correspond to the variation in the Sun's distance from the observer as the Sun spirals toward and away from the observer's disc radius.

A separate and more diagnostically interesting effect is the Sun's apparent angular diameter variation during the day: the Sun's apparent diameter at the horizon is greater than at zenith in certain atmospheric conditions ("setting sun illusion"). On the spheroidal model, this is a purely psychological/optical illusion (the Ponzo effect). On Model C, if the Sun is a local luminary at finite altitude and moves radially across the disc toward the observer's horizon, its angular diameter should decrease as it approaches the horizon (the Sun is retreating to a farther horizontal distance). The observation that the sun does not dramatically change size during its daily motion is therefore a mild challenge for Model C if the local altitude is very low.

Sunrise, Sunset, and Hotspot Illumination

On Model C, the Sun illuminates a circular region of the disc surface. The boundary of that region corresponds to the "terminator" — the day-night boundary. As the Sun spirals outward toward the equatorial region (southern summer for disc-edge observers), the illuminated circle expands; as it spirals inward (northern summer), the illuminated circle contracts from the disc edge. The terminator is thus a circle on the disc whose radius depends on the Sun's spiral position.

The observed pattern of day length across latitudes — longer summer days at higher northern latitudes, continuous daylight at the North Pole in summer, and the corresponding shortening of days as one approaches the disc edge in the "southern" direction — is qualitatively consistent with this spiral illumination model.

Quantitatively: on the disc model, an observer at distance r from the center, with the Sun at altitude h_sun and in a circular orbit of radius r_sun at that moment, is in daylight if and only if the Sun is within the circle of visibility from the observer's position. The day-length calculation becomes a geometric problem in two-dimensional disc geometry, solvable in closed form. The predicted day-length as a function of observer distance from the center and time of year (Sun's orbit radius) can be compared to the observed day-lengths. A detailed calibration is given in § 6.7.

Water Finds Its Level

The Physical Claim

The statement "water finds its level" expresses the classical observation that water in a connected container seeks a state of minimum potential energy, which on a flat non-rotating surface corresponds to a horizontal plane. On a rotating spheroid, the "level" surface is an equipotential that is curved — but the curvature is a consequence of the gravitational and centrifugal potential, not an independent fact. On a flat disc without rotation, the equipotential surface is flat.

The large-scale bodies of water on Earth — lakes, seas, enclosed bodies — provide in principle a direct test. On the spheroid model, a long lake or canal should have measurable curvature: the middle of the lake is higher than the ends (measured orthogonally to the gravitational field) by:

\[ \Delta h = \frac{L^2}{8R} \]

For a lake of length L = 100 km, \(\Delta h = \frac{(10^5)^2}{8 \times 6.371 \times 10^6} \approx 196 \, \text{m}\).

This is a substantial elevation difference. However, the standard response is that this is not a "real" elevation difference in the geodetic sense: the water surface is the reference level (the geoid), and any instrument measuring "height above sea level" uses the geoid as its reference. The 196-meter difference is real in the Cartesian sense but is by definition zero in the geodetic sense, because geodetic height is defined relative to the curved geoid.

This is another instance of the circularity identified by criterion (e): the observation that a lake is "at the same elevation" throughout presupposes that "elevation" is measured relative to the curved geoid, which presupposes the spheroidal model.

Engineering Evidence

The Suez Canal (193 km) and the Panama Canal were both built as sea-level waterways (the Panama Canal uses locks for its non-sea-level sections, but the sea-level sections at each end are at ocean level). On the spheroid, the sea-level surface is curved, and the canal follows this curved surface; no curvature "bulge" is visible because the canal is part of the curved surface. On the disc model, the sea-level surface is flat, and the canal follows this flat surface.

Both accounts predict the same observable fact: the canal is at sea level throughout the sea-level sections. The observation does not discriminate between the models.

Surveyors' Evidence and the Geodetic Correction

In classical land surveying over long distances, surveyors apply a "curvature and refraction" correction to their level readings. Over a 10 km distance, the combined correction is approximately 13 cm (9 cm for curvature, 4 cm for atmospheric refraction in the opposite direction). These corrections are applied routinely in precision leveling and are said to be confirmed by their consistency with other measurements.

The correction is an auxiliary hypothesis applied within the spheroidal framework. Whether it represents a genuine geometric correction or a convention adopted to maintain internal consistency of the spheroidal coordinate system cannot be determined by the correction itself. This is, again, an application of the circularity criterion.

Cartographic Survival

The UN Azimuthal Equidistant Projection

The emblem of the United Nations, adopted in 1946, uses a polar azimuthal equidistant projection centered on the North Pole. This projection places the North Pole at the center and the equator at radius R; parallels are equally spaced concentric circles; meridians are equally spaced radial lines. The same projection appears in the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) aeronautical charts for polar navigation.

The azimuthal equidistant projection is the correct map for a flat disc centered on the North Pole: it would be a geometrically faithful representation if the Earth's surface were such a disc. On the spheroidal model, the projection is a useful cartographic convention for certain purposes (equal radial distances from the center are correctly represented), but it distorts shapes and areas as one moves away from the center point.

The choice of this projection for global navigation charts (ICAO DOC 8697, Aeronautical Chart Manual) is cited as circumstantial evidence for Model C: why would the aviation authority responsible for global navigation adopt a projection that corresponds to the disc model's geometry for its most globally comprehensive charts? The standard response is that the azimuthal equidistant projection has practical navigational virtues for polar routes (great circles appear as approximately straight lines from the pole) that justify its use. This explanation is technically valid.

Aviation Route Patterns Revisited

The ICAO North Atlantic Organized Track System (NAT-OTS) routes and the polar North Pacific routes (PACOTS) are organized on a spheroidal framework. The actual routes flown by commercial aircraft across the North Pole region — from North America to Asia — are planned using great-circle orthodromes and match predictions from the spheroidal geometry well.

For transpolar routes in the Southern Hemisphere, the same analysis would predict direct routes across Antarctica. The ICAO rules for Polar operations (ICAO Doc 9574) do not prohibit Antarctic overflight but specify enhanced equipment requirements. The absence of commercial service across Antarctica is therefore not a regulatory prohibition but a commercial and operational choice. Model C advocates must acknowledge that this absence is not conclusive evidence; it is consistent with either model.

Gnomonic Projection and Great Circles

On the gnomonic projection — centered on any selected point, with the property that great circles on the sphere appear as straight lines — a navigator can plot the great-circle route by drawing a straight line between two points. This projection is used for route planning precisely because of this property.

On the disc model, the "straight line" route between two points is the straight line on the disc: there is no great-circle vs. rhumb-line distinction. The gnomonic projection coincides with the disc itself for routes passing through the projection's center. For routes not through the center, the gnomonic projection predicts paths that curve on the disc — consistent with the observed curvature of airline tracks on flat maps.

The two geometric frameworks make the same prediction for many route geometries precisely because the disc model (on the azimuthal equidistant projection) and the spheroidal model (on the gnomonic projection from the North Pole) produce the same qualitative route patterns for the vast majority of northern hemisphere aviation.

§ 7.1 The Argument Summarized

This monograph has advanced a coherent, multi-layered argument. It may be summarized in six propositions:

P1Epistemological

The conditions for genuine knowledge (§ 1.1) include stable justification under substitution of methodology, non-circularity, and independence from the conclusion being established. These are high standards, but they are the standards that distinguish genuine knowledge from well-supported opinion.

P2Modality

Claims about large-scale terrestrial geometry rest primarily on instrument-mediated data and mathematical modeling — the two modalities with the largest auxiliary requirements and the most significant theory-ladenness. No human being has directly observed the Earth's shape as a whole; all such knowledge is inferential.

P3Underdetermination

Multiple models — the oblate spheroid (A), the neo-Tychonic system (B), the bounded disc (C), the concave sphere (D), and the simulation model (E) — are empirically equivalent over large domains of observation. The empirical equivalence of A and D (through the inversion transformation) is mathematically exact; the equivalence of A and B is historically demonstrated; the equivalence of A and C over accessible observational domains is argued throughout Part IV.

P4Institutional

The mechanisms of paradigm formation, peer review, grant funding, and career incentives produce a structural tendency toward consensus stabilization independent of unique determination by evidence. The replication crisis across psychology and biomedical science establishes that published consensus can be systematically wrong. These structural features apply to any scientific domain, including the domain of geodesy and geophysics.

P5Affirmative

Positive evidence for Model C includes: the Michelson-Morley-Miller residual's sidereal periodicity, which is not consistent with simple thermal artifact but is consistent with a Lorentzian aether in a frame where the Earth's surface is stationary (§ 6.2); the quantitative predictions of Model C's disc geometry for day length, stellar pole positions, and horizon behavior that match observational patterns without unique failure (§§ 6.3, 6.4, 6.7); the cartographic and navigational conventions that are formally equivalent to disc-model predictions over the accessible range of aviation and navigation (§ 6.6); and the circumstantial evidence of differential access to the most model-discriminating geographic region (§ 6.1).

P6Burden

The burden of proof on any affirmative empirical claim lies with its proposer. The proposition "the Earth is an oblate spheroid" is an affirmative empirical claim. This monograph has argued that the evidence for this claim, while substantial, does not uniquely force the conclusion over all competing models, is in significant part circular or model-dependent, and is embedded in a scientific framework (ΛCDM cosmology) that is currently exhibiting the signs of a degenerating research programme. The burden has not been discharged.

§ 7.2 What Would Falsify Model C

A serious researcher must state the conditions under which their own thesis would be abandoned. The following conditions would constitute genuine falsifying evidence for Model C, in the strong sense:

F1Independent Geodetic Survey.

A geodetic survey of the Antarctic region conducted by an independent organization — not funded by or logistically dependent on existing Treaty-signatory national programs — using independent navigation (inertial navigation systems cross-checked by astronomical observation, not GPS) that: (a) demonstrates continuous passable terrain connecting what are claimed to be antipodal points in the Antarctic; (b) establishes that the distance between Sydney and Buenos Aires via the South Pole is approximately 11,000 km (consistent with spheroidal geometry) rather than the much larger distance predicted by the azimuthal equidistant disc model; and (c) confirms by direct astronomical observation that the South Celestial Pole exists as a fixed point in the sky above a specific geographical location on the Antarctic continent. The key word is "independent": the survey must use methodology and logistics that do not presuppose the spheroidal model.

F2Free Circumnavigation of Antarctica.

A privately funded, independently navigated maritime circumnavigation of Antarctica — without logistical support from national Antarctic programs — that: (a) maintains continuous independent verification of position by celestial navigation; (b) demonstrates that the circumnavigation distance is approximately 14,000–17,000 km (consistent with the perimeter of a continent of 14 million km²) rather than approximately 40,000 km or more (consistent with a global ice frontier on the disc model); and (c) conducts regular depth soundings and ice margin measurements sufficient to characterize the extent of the ice barrier. This would straightforwardly discriminate between the continent model and the ice-frontier model.

F3Thermal Resolution of the Miller Residual.

A complete thermal resolution of the Michelson-Morley-Miller residual that: (a) reproduces Miller's observed fringe-shift amplitude and sidereal periodicity using a purely thermal simulation; (b) explains specifically why the thermal artifact has sidereal (23h 56m) rather than solar (24h 00m) period, given that temperature cycles in a canvas enclosure are driven by solar heating; and (c) demonstrates that the predicted sidereal period of thermal artifacts at the specific geographic location (Mount Wilson, latitude 34°N) and time of year of Miller's measurements matches the observed phase of the fringe shift. Shankland's 1955 analysis did not fully satisfy condition (b); a complete thermal resolution would.

F4Dark Matter Detection.

A direct detection of dark matter — the identification of a specific particle species with the mass, interaction cross-sections, and spatial distribution required by ΛCDM — in two or more independent experiments using different detection technologies. This would remove the most significant open auxiliary hypothesis in Model A's framework and strengthen the Lakatosian progressiveness of the ΛCDM programme.

F5Hubble Tension Resolution Without Modification.

A demonstrated resolution of the Hubble tension — H₀ > 5σ discrepancy between CMB-based and local distance-ladder measurements — that does not require any modification of the ΛCDM hard core, achieved through demonstrated systematic error in one of the measurement chains. This would show that the degenerating symptoms identified in § 2.4 are artifacts rather than genuine anomalies.

§ 7.3 The Burden

The foregoing argument may be stated as a syllogism:

Major premise: An empirical proposition is known only when its supporting evidence does not depend on the conclusion being established, is stable across independent methodologies, and survives the underdetermination test (i.e., the evidence uniquely supports the proposition over its competitors).

Minor premise: The proposition "the Earth is an oblate spheroid" is supported by evidence that (a) is significantly circular — GPS coordinates, satellite imagery, and geodetic surveys all presuppose the spheroidal framework; (b) is not stable across independent methodologies — the Lakatosian embedding framework (ΛCDM) exhibits degenerating anomalies; and (c) does not survive the underdetermination test — Models B, C, and D are empirically equivalent to Model A over the domains most accessible to direct investigation.

Conclusion: The proposition "the Earth is an oblate spheroid" is not known in the strict sense defined by this monograph. It is one option within a set of empirically distinguishable but unequally-tested models, and the preference for the orthodox model is conventional, sustained by institutional structure, and not uniquely forced by the available evidence.

The proposition "the Earth is an oblate spheroid" is an affirmative empirical claim. This monograph has argued that the burden of proof has not been discharged by its proposers; it has been presupposed. Until that burden is discharged by independent evidence — evidence that does not itself depend on the conclusion — the proposition remains one option within a set of empirically equivalent models, and the preference for the orthodox model is conventional, not forced.
