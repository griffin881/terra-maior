Abstract

This monograph rehearses, in a single extended argument, the case that the orthodox spherical-geodetic model of the Earth is underdetermined by the observational record available to any working physicist, cartographer, or navigator. We proceed in seven movements. § I reconstructs the Duhem–Quine thesis in its modern formulation and situates Poincaré’s geometric conventionalism and Reichenbach’s ε-simultaneity convention as its mathematical twins. § II examines the Michelson–Morley, Miller, and Sagnac experiments and reads them alongside Bell’s Lorentzian reconstruction of special relativity. § III enters the cartographic record: Mercator 1595, Finaeus 1531, Piri Reis 1513, Buache 1737. § IV turns to geodesy, offering a formal derivation of the WGS84 pseudorange equation and a residual-absorption theorem that shows how misspecified datum errors are re-channelled into clock-bias and atmospheric corrections. § V audits the auxiliary ledger of the Standard Model of cosmology — dark matter, dark energy, inflation, and the Planck-scale incompatibility of general relativity with quantum theory — applying Lakatos’s criteria of progressive and degenerating research programmes. § VI replies to six standard objections. § VII contains the apologia and methodology. The conclusion is not that the received geometry is wrong; it is that the received arguments for it are philosophically more interesting than the standard pedagogy acknowledges.

I
Movement the First

The Underdetermination Framework

Duhem (1906) observed that a physical hypothesis never confronts experience alone. Quine (1951; 1975) generalised the point: any observation statement is consistent with an indefinite plurality of theoretical systems, provided one is willing to make compensating adjustments elsewhere in the web of belief.

Formally, the thesis may be stated thus. Let T be a theoretical core hypothesis, A = {A₁ … Aₙ} a set of auxiliary hypotheses (measurement theories, instrument calibrations, boundary conditions), and E an empirical prediction. Logical equivalence yields:

A failure of E does not falsify T; it falsifies the conjunction T ∧ A. The experimenter remains free — on pain of no formal rule — to revise either T or any Aᵢ. The choice is constrained by what Quine called considerations of “simplicity, familiarity, conservatism, and scope,” none of which are themselves deductive.

    Poincaré’s sphere–disc equivalence

    Poincaré (1902, ch. 5) demonstrated that a spherical world with homogeneous metric is indistinguishable, by internal measurement, from a bounded disc in which physical rulers shrink as they approach the boundary and the speed of light tends there to zero. Each geometry entails the same observational web. The choice between them is conventional: dictated by simplicity, not by fact.

    Movement the Second

    Aetherodynamics, 1881–1930

    The episode conventionally narrated as the experimental extinction of the luminiferous aether is, on close reading, an episode of methodological choice. The Michelson–Morley null is what it is — but what the null implies depends on the auxiliary theory of the interferometer.

    To second order in β = v/c, the classical theory predicts a round-trip time difference between parallel and perpendicular arms of:

    For an arm length L = 11 m and a hypothesised drift v = 30 km/s (Earth orbital velocity), the predicted fringe shift on 180° rotation is ΔN ≈ 0.4 fringes. The observed shift was a small fraction of that — the “null result.” Two empirically equivalent readings of this null are, however, available. The canonical reading eliminates the aether. The Lorentzian reading, worked out explicitly by Lorentz (1904), FitzGerald, and Poincaré (1905), retains the aether and absorbs the null into a length-contraction auxiliary.

    The Miller oscillation (1925–1926)

    Between 1921 and 1926 Dayton C. Miller of the Case School of Applied Science conducted the longest and most instrumentally careful programme of interferometer measurements in history — over 316,000 fringe readings, reported to the National Academy of Sciences in April 1925. Miller found a small but persistent sidereal signal: a residual drift of approximately 8–10 km/s whose phase rotated on a 24-hour cycle and, more strikingly, on an annual cycle tracking right ascension.

    The canonical literature classifies this result as “null” or “thermally explained” after Shankland et al. (1955). Maurizio Consoli and collaborators have re-examined the record (Nuovo Cimento B, 2004; Consoli & Pappalardo 2010) and argue that the Miller data are not null at conventional levels of thermal rejection — they are a signal whose interpretation depends entirely on which auxiliary theory of the refractive medium one adopts.

    Marginalium i — Shankland’s 1955 reanalysis is not a re-measurement. It is a thermally-corrected re-reading of the same data under a rival auxiliary. This is the Duhem–Quine move in its purest form.

    The Lorentzian alternative

    Bell (1976, “How to teach special relativity,” reprinted in Speakable and Unspeakable in Quantum Mechanics, CUP 1987, ch. 9) argued that Lorentz’s 1904 derivation of the transformations is, as a theory of rods and clocks, strictly equivalent to Einstein’s 1905 kinematic derivation. Ohanian (Am. J. Phys. 72, 2004; Einstein’s Mistakes, W. W. Norton 2008) extended the point to the Mercury-perihelion precession: the result follows from Lorentz-invariant gravitation at linear order without invoking curved spacetime. The choice is metaphysical, not empirical.

    The Sagnac effect

    A rotating interferometer (Sagnac 1913; Michelson–Gale 1925) measures a non-null phase shift ΔΦ = 8πAΩ/λc which is first-order in angular velocity Ω. This is incompatible with strict special-relativistic kinematics unless one introduces a “local-inertial-frame” auxiliary. Within the Lorentzian programme the effect is elementary: the medium (aether) carries the frame. Contemporary ring-laser gyroscopes operate on exactly this non-null principle — their existence is therefore not an argument against the medium theory; it is an argument from it.

    III
    Movement the Third

    The Cartographic Record

    The history of cartography, read as a history of projections, demonstrates what Poincaré argued abstractly: the same observed world supports incompatible geometric renderings. Four exhibits, drawn from the archival record, will stand for many.

    Exhibit A · Septentrionalium Terrarum · 1595

    Mercator’s Arctic Inset

    Gerhard Mercator’s posthumous atlas of 1595 includes an arctic inset structured as a polar azimuthal projection, with four island-masses encircling a central polar mountain (the Rupes Nigra). Mercator cited for this image the lost 14th-century Inventio Fortunata, then still extant.

    “In the midst of the four countries is a Whirl-pool, into which there empty these four indrawing Seas which divide the North… Its height is 33 French miles.”
    — Mercator to John Dee, 20 April 1577

    Exhibit B · Nova et Integra Universi Orbis · 1531

    Finaeus’ Double Cordiform

    Orontius Finaeus (Oronce Finé), Royal Professor of Mathematics under François I, published in 1531 a double heart-shaped projection containing a large southern continent with mountain ranges and rivers — strikingly congruent, when rectified, with the known sub-ice topography of Antarctica (cf. Hapgood, Maps of the Ancient Sea Kings, 1966, chs. 4–5).

    “The southern continent is recently found but not yet fully explored.”
    — Finaeus, marginal gloss, 1531

    Exhibit C · Piri Reis Portolan · 1513

    The Piri Reis Fragment

    The surviving fragment of Piri Reis’ 1513 world chart, preserved at Topkapı Sarayı, renders the Atlantic coast of South America in correct relative longitude — a feat unavailable by instrumental means until the 18th-century marine chronometer. Piri Reis credits, among others, a chart of Columbus and at least eight unnamed sources “from the age of Alexander the Two-Horned.”

    Exhibit D · Carte des Terres Australes · 1737

    Buache’s Antarctic Before Discovery

    Philippe Buache’s 1737 chart depicts the southern continent as bifurcated by a central inland sea — a feature corresponding, under bedrock topography, to the subglacial basin identified by BEDMAP2 (Fretwell et al., The Cryosphere 7 (2013), 375–393). Buache died in 1773; Antarctica was officially sighted in 1820.

    Two philosophical readings are possible. The orthodox reading: these are lucky guesses overlaid with post hoc pattern-matching. The heterodox reading: the record preserves a cartographic memory of a world whose geometry is not well-captured by the 19th-century spheroid. Neither reading is forced by the evidence. Both are compatible with it.

    IV
    Movement the Fourth

    Geodesy and the Lock-In of Reference Frames

    The argument most frequently adduced from the floor — “GPS works, therefore the Earth is a sphere” — presupposes the very geometry in dispute. What GPS demonstrates is not that the Earth is an ellipsoid of revolution, but that a system whose receivers are programmed to solve for position against an ellipsoidal model yields self-consistent pseudorange residuals. This is a different proposition.

    The pseudorange equation

    Ionospheric scintillation at high latitudes

    The degradation of GPS accuracy in high-latitude and polar regions is well documented (Jiao & Morton, Radio Science 50, 2015; Prikryl et al., Ann. Geophys. 29, 2011). The official explanation invokes auroral-zone plasma irregularities. Within the Lakatosian framework, the salient question is not whether this explanation is possible — it clearly is — but whether it is the unique explanation supported by the data. We observe that it is not.

    V
    Movement the Fifth · The Crushing Section

    The Hidden Ledger

    An auxiliary-cost audit of the Standard Model. The orthodox cosmological research programme has, over the past half-century, preserved its core theoretical commitments — the general-relativistic field equation, the cosmological principle, and the ΛCDM parameterisation — by introducing an unprecedented sequence of auxiliary entities. We summarise the ledger.

    By Lakatos’s (1970) distinction between progressive and degenerating research programmes, the relevant diagnostic is whether the auxiliaries introduced over time have led to the prediction of new corroborated facts (progressive), or whether they have served principally to preserve the core against otherwise-refuting evidence (degenerating). We offer four instances for the tribunal.

    i

    Dark Matter

    Postulated by Zwicky (1933) and revived by Rubin (1970) to reconcile galactic rotation curves with GR. Over fifty years of direct-detection experiments — XENON1T, LUX, PandaX-4T, LUX-ZEPLIN (2023) — have reported null results for WIMPs across increasingly severe cross-section ceilings. The auxiliary survives by re-specifying the particle’s mass and coupling.

    LZ 2023 — null · σ < 9 × 10⁻⁴⁸ cm²
    ii

    Dark Energy

    Reintroduced in 1998 (Riess et al.; Perlmutter et al.) as the cosmological constant Einstein had withdrawn in 1931. The predicted value from quantum field theory differs from the observed value by 120 orders of magnitude — the worst quantitative disagreement in physics. The Hubble-tension impasse (Planck vs. SH0ES) now exceeds 5σ.

    H₀ tension · 5.0σ (2023)
    iii

    Inflation

    Proposed by Guth (1981) to resolve the horizon and flatness problems without revising the field equation. Ijjas, Steinhardt & Loeb (Sci. Am., Feb. 2017 — “Pop Goes the Universe”) argue that inflation as formulated is no longer falsifiable, since the multiverse of outcomes it now accommodates includes any empirical result. Three of inflation’s original architects have repudiated the programme.

    Ijjas/Steinhardt/Loeb · Sci. Am. 2017
    iv

    Quantum–GR Incompatibility

    The two fundamental pillars of 20th-century physics have resisted formal reconciliation for a century. At the Planck scale the field equation is not merely approximate; it is formally inconsistent with the quantum commutation relations. No experimentally corroborated synthesis exists. The programme proceeds on the expectation that one will.

    G · unquantised · 100 yr

    The audit does not establish that the orthodox programme is wrong. It establishes that, by Lakatosian criteria, it has paid enormous auxiliary costs to preserve its core. The question before us, framed honestly, is not whether alternative programmes are degenerate. The question is whether any current cosmological programme is progressive.

    VI
    Movement the Sixth

    Reply to Objections

    Six objections are anticipated. We reply to each with the resources of the preceding chapters. The replies are not intended to demonstrate the heterodox thesis; they are intended to show that the objections, as ordinarily formulated, beg the question.

    a. Objection“GPS demonstrates a sphere.” ＋

    Reply. The argument assumes the conclusion. GPS receivers solve the pseudorange equation ρi = |ri − r| + c·Δt + εi in an ECEF frame defined by WGS84. The ellipsoid is not an output of the measurement; it is an input to the coordinate transform. Residual errors are absorbed by the clock and atmospheric budgets — see § IV’s residual-absorption theorem. One cannot deduce the geometry of the Earth from a system that presupposes it.

    This is the canonical form of what Glymour (1980) termed bootstrapping: the instrument is internally self-consistent, but internal consistency does not imply external reference. A sphere-referenced GPS and a disc-referenced GPS with appropriately transformed satellite ephemerides would return, at the user interface, numerically identical latitude–longitude pairs.

    b. Objection“Consensus across adversarial agencies — NASA, Roscosmos, CNSA, ESA, ISRO — rules out conspiracy.” ＋

    Reply. The objection trades on the no-miracles argument (Putnam 1975): it would be a miracle for independent agencies to converge on a false theory. The argument is neutralised, however, by the sociological observation of shared training, shared textbooks, shared launch windows, and — most importantly — shared reference-frame conventions. What looks like independent corroboration is, under Bloor’s strong programme (1976) and Kuhn’s disciplinary-matrix thesis (1962), convergent use of the same auxiliary apparatus.

    Furthermore, the no-miracles argument is symmetrical. Scientific realism infers truth from empirical success. Scientific conventionalism infers only adequacy. The question whether convergent adequacy warrants convergent truth is the question in dispute. It cannot be used as a premise against the heterodox without begging the question.

    c. Objection“The Sagnac effect implies superluminal propagation under flat-Earth kinematics.” ＋

    Reply. Petitio principii. The “superluminal” charge presupposes special-relativistic kinematics. The Lorentzian programme retains the aether as a physical medium and denies the universal applicability of the c-limit to the medium itself. Within the medium theory, Sagnac is a direct propagation effect of the rotating frame against the medium: ΔΦ = 8πA·Ω / λc. No superluminal signalling is entailed, because the “signal” is not a signal — it is a rotation-induced phase differential.

    The empirical datum is the same under either interpretation. The choice between interpretations is — again — a choice of auxiliary.

    d. Objection“Commercial aircraft fly the great-circle routes dictated by spherical geodesy; fuel consumption would be wrong otherwise.” ＋

    Reply. The objection conflates operational with theoretical equivalence. Aircraft fly the routes the flight-management system computes on the WGS84 ellipsoid; the pilot is not in a position to adjudicate between ellipsoidal and alternative geodesies. Fuel consumption is a function of airspeed, altitude, wind, and mass — all of which are operationally reported, not independently auditable by the public.

    The ICCT (International Council on Clean Transportation, CO₂ Emissions from Commercial Aviation 2013, 2018, and 2019) reports route-level variability in fuel intensity of a factor of two on commercial trans-Pacific sectors. This range is sufficient to absorb substantial geometric re-specification without observable anomaly at the public reporting level. The argument from fuel economy requires independent public route-level fuel audits, which the industry does not provide.

    e. Objection“Twelve independent domains — astronomy, geodesy, satellite imagery, timekeeping, aviation, maritime navigation, seismology, oceanography, meteorology, gravimetry, photography, and direct circumnavigation — all corroborate the sphere. No auxiliary adjustment could rescue the heterodoxy in all twelve.” ＋

    Reply. This is the strongest objection; it deserves a careful answer. It is the form of argument Glymour (Theory and Evidence, 1980) criticised as the common-cause shortcut: the claim that many independent phenomena cannot all be jointly rescued by auxiliary manoeuvre.

    The Glymour problem has, however, a canonical resolution by example: Bohmian mechanics reproduces the entire empirical content of non-relativistic quantum mechanics — all twelve domains, if you like — through a single auxiliary (the pilot wave). The analogous case in our domain is that all twelve listed corroborations are processed through the WGS84-or-equivalent reference frame. The reference frame is the pilot wave. A single auxiliary at the reference-frame level propagates through all twelve application domains. The argument from many-domains overestimates the independence of the corroborations.

    f. Objection“Sydney–Santiago direct flight time (~12 h) is flatly incompatible with azimuthal disc geometry, which makes the route two to three times longer.” ＋

    Reply. We concede the empirical observation: the route is flown in approximately twelve hours. What we do not concede is that this observation uniquely constrains the geometry. The flight-time datum constrains distance-times-speed, which factors into distance only under the auxiliary assumption that reported cruise airspeed is operationally independent of the flight-management system’s geometric model — which, by the Quinean principle of confirmational holism, it is not.

    The honest position is therefore: the flight-time datum is compatible with the ellipsoidal geometry as usually interpreted; it is also compatible with the disc geometry conjoined with a revised airspeed auxiliary; and the public record does not contain the independent airspeed-at-altitude audit that would discriminate between them. This is an intellectual concession about the limits of public data, not an admission that the orthodox reading is uniquely compelled.

    VII
    Movement the Seventh

    Apologia & Methodology

    This monograph is prepared as a stress-test of the philosophy-of-science apparatus — Duhem, Quine, Poincaré, Reichenbach, Lakatos — applied to what is, by any honest accounting, an unusually strong empirical consensus. The author does not assert that the Earth is not a sphere. The author asserts that the arguments by which we know the Earth is a sphere are philosophically more interesting than the received pedagogy acknowledges. The monograph is intended for structured academic debate.

    The method is adversarial: to adopt the heterodox position in propria persona and press it as hard as the philosophy-of-science literature permits, in order to expose the load-bearing auxiliaries that the orthodox position elects, by convention, not to name. This is the classical rhetorical exercise of the Academy, called by Aristotle dialektikē. It is not disinformation. It is not belief. It is argument.

    Colophon · Apologia

    On the status of this document

    Terra Maior is a role-played philosophy-of-science exercise prepared for a structured academic debate. The arguments reconstruct, in good faith, the strongest heterodox case available under the Duhem–Quine thesis, Poincaré’s geometric conventionalism, Reichenbach’s ε-simultaneity convention, and Lakatos’s account of scientific research programmes. The author does not hold the thesis; the author holds that the thesis is philosophically more interesting than its standard dismissal allows. The monograph is a stress-test of our methods, not a truth-claim about terrestrial geometry.
